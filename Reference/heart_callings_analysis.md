# Heart Reference Callings — Pattern Analysis

This document analyses only the five Heart: The City Beneath base callings (Adventure, Enlightenment, Forced, Heartsong, Penitent) for structural patterns and design rules. It is intentionally isolated from the Shackles v5 ideation material so it can be used as a clean baseline for v2 comparison work.

The five callings examined: **Adventure · Enlightenment · Forced · Heartsong · Penitent**

---

## 1. Structure

Every calling uses the same skeleton in the same order:

1. Name
2. Italic flavour text
3. `## Core Ability` → `### ABILITY NAME` (all caps)
4. `## Character Creation Questions`
5. `## Minor Beats`
6. `## Major Beats`
7. `## Zenith Beats`
8. `## Calling Equipment`

No calling deviates from this order. No section is omitted. There are no sub-sections within the beat tiers.

---

## 2. Flavour Text

The opening flavour text is always italic, always 2–3 sentences, always addressed directly to the player ("You don't want to be down here"). It never exceeds roughly 50–60 words.

The structure of the flavour text follows a consistent pattern across all five:

**Sentence 1:** State the condition — what is true of this person right now. *(You don't want to be down here. / Everyone said what you intended to do was impossible. / The City Above holds no more excitement for you.)*

**Sentence 2:** State the drive — what they are doing about it or why they are here. *(You're looking for secrets hidden deep within the earth. / In the City Beneath, on the knife-edge between real and unreal, you can be who you really are.)*

**Sentence 3 (often):** Name the cost or the complication — the thing that makes the calling tragic rather than triumphant. *(No matter what it takes, you'll get them. / The question is only what you spend in the pursuit of the next thing.)*

The flavour text never describes mechanics and never refers to beats or abilities. It is pure character positioning.

---

## 3. Core Ability

### Naming
All five ability names are in ALL CAPS: COLLATERAL, UNORTHODOX METHODS, LEGENDARY, NOT YET, IN THE BLOOD.

The name is always followed by an italic flavour sentence before the mechanical text. The flavour sentence is always a short characterisation of the ability in fiction — what it *means* rather than what it *does*.

### Mechanical Structures
The five core abilities use three distinct structures:

**Once per session, active effect:**
- COLLATERAL: allocate your own stress to a nearby target instead of marking it yourself
- NOT YET: avoid negative effects from Blood or Mind fallout for the remainder of the situation
- UNORTHODOX METHODS (partial): state that your result is a 6 before rolling — you succeed but take stress

**Always-on passive effect:**
- LEGENDARY: when you gain a minor advance, refresh D6. When you gain a major advance, refresh D8.
- IN THE BLOOD: +1 Echo Protection. Once per *situation*, allocate stress to Echo.

Note: IN THE BLOOD uses "once per situation" for its active component, not "once per session" — this is the only calling to use this timing. The distinction matters: situations are shorter than sessions, so it fires more often.

**Combination (passive skill grant + once per session active):**
- UNORTHODOX METHODS: Gain the Discern skill. Once per session, [active effect].

UNORTHODOX METHODS is the only core ability that grants a named skill. This is notable: in Heart's class system, skills are advance currency. Granting one in the core ability is a meaningful power signal.

### Mechanical Scope
All five core abilities do exactly one thing per clause. None introduce sub-systems or require additional rolls. The longest is LEGENDARY at two sentences; the shortest is COLLATERAL at one.

**LEGENDARY is the structural outlier.** It is the only core ability with no session or situation trigger — it fires at character advancement milestones (when you gain an advance), not during play. Every other ability fires during a session. This is unique to Adventure, which is thematically about the accumulation of glory through play rather than a specific dramatic moment.

## 4. Character Creation Questions

### Count
Four questions in all five callings. **Forced has five** — the only exception. The extra question in Forced ("Choose another player character. They have history with your masters too.") is structurally the same as a standard PC bond question but is added on top of four questions that deal exclusively with the calling's antagonist (the masters). The exception is intentional: Forced is a calling defined by external control, so the questions are front-loaded with antagonist detail before adding a PC relationship.

### PC Bond Questions
All five callings include at least one question that references another PC directly, using the formula "Choose another player character." Most include two such questions.

- Adventure: 2 PC bond questions (shared danger; shared item dispute)
- Enlightenment: 2 PC bond questions (invaluable ally; PC hiding secrets)
- Forced: 1 PC bond question (they have history with your masters)
- Heartsong: 1 PC bond question (shared unearthly sight)
- Penitent: 2 PC bond questions (present at your betrayal; someone you look up to)

The PC bond questions always describe a *specific past event or relationship* rather than asking a general "who is your friend?" question. The event or dynamic described should appear in the beats — if it doesn't, the question is wasted.

### Motivation Questions
Every calling includes at least one question that grounds the core motivation:
- What "impossible" thing are you attempting? (Enlightenment)
- What drove you out of the City Above? (Adventure)
- Who are your masters? (Forced) — technically antagonist, not motivation, but establishes the condition
- Which three images do you repeatedly see when you dream? (Heartsong) — sensory, not goal-oriented

Some callings ask for a goal ("what are you attempting?"); others ask for an origin ("what drove you?"); Heartsong asks for sensory texture ("which images?"). The approach varies by calling type.

### World-Visibility Questions
At least one question per calling establishes something already visible in the world — a consequence or mark that exists before the campaign begins:
- What evidence of your failings is visible in the City Beneath? (Penitent) — direct
- What's the most dangerous beast you've heard tell of? (Adventure) — implies existing reputation
- How does your connection to the Heart manifest physically? (Heartsong) — a visible mark on the body
- How are your masters maintaining power over you? (Forced) — an existing mechanism

These questions establish hooks that the GM can use immediately, rather than requiring the player to build backstory in a vacuum.

## 5. Minor Beats

### Count
| Calling | Minor Beat Count |
|---|---|
| Adventure | 21 |
| Enlightenment | 19 |
| Forced | 20 |
| Heartsong | 23 |
| Penitent | 21 |

Range: 19–23. Every calling sits in this band. Heartsong is the largest at 23; Enlightenment is the smallest at 19.

### Fallout Beats
Every calling includes at least one "Take [Level] [Track] fallout" beat in the minor tier. This is the only type of beat where the game's mechanical systems are explicitly named.

**Critical exception: Adventure.** Adventure lists *Major* Blood fallout and *Major* Fortune fallout in its *minor* beats. No other calling does this. This is a deliberate design signal: Adventure is a calling about spectacular, immediate recklessness. The ordinary beats of this life carry catastrophic costs. Adventure does not have separate major fallout beats in its major tier as a result — the escalation pattern is front-loaded.

Fallout tracks by calling:
- Forced: Fortune (minor) — material/luck domain
- Enlightenment: Mind (minor), Supplies (minor) — cognitive and material, both
- Adventure: Major Blood (minor), Major Fortune (minor) — physical and luck, both, at major level
- Penitent: Blood (minor) — physical
- Heartsong: Echo (minor) — supernatural

**Enlightenment is the only calling with two different fallout tracks in the minor tier.** This reflects the dual cost of pursuing forbidden knowledge: it costs your mind and your resources simultaneously.

### Required Beat Types
Across all five callings, every minor beat set includes representatives of the following categories:

**The obsession in action** — the character actively doing the thing their calling compels them toward. *"Put the acquisition of knowledge above preserving the lives of your allies" (Enlightenment). "Rush into danger before anyone else" (Adventure). "Follow orders from your masters, even though they put you at risk" (Forced).*

**The tension beat** — a beat that contradicts or complicates the calling's drive. This is always paired with an "obsession in action" beat to form a tension pair. *"Rebel against your orders, even though this puts you at risk" (Forced). "Do something your order would frown upon" (Penitent). "Flee from combat or a similarly dangerous situation" (Enlightenment — the seeker who runs).*

**The NPC relationship beat** — forge, damage, lose, or leverage a bond with a named NPC. *"Forge a friendship or romantic relationship with an NPC" (Forced). "Acquire an NPC bond who has access to useful information" (Enlightenment). "Acquire an NPC bond who belongs to your order" (Penitent). "Perform an act of service to an NPC witch" (Heartsong).*

**The consequence beat** — the calling making life difficult for the character or those around them. *"Get into trouble as someone discovers your chequered past" (Forced). "Let your curiosity lead you into danger" (Heartsong). "Damage or sabotage a haven, letting the Heart in" (Heartsong).*

**The revelation beat** — the calling's backstory surfacing, a secret shared, the past made visible. *"Allude to the events that led you to seek forbidden knowledge" (Enlightenment). "Witness first-hand the tragic extent of your failings" (Penitent). "Get into trouble as someone discovers your chequered past" (Forced).*

### Tension Pairs
Every calling has at least one explicit tension pair — two beats describing opposite responses to the same pressure. Forced has the most: three tension pairs across its twenty beats.

- Forced: "Follow orders even at risk" / "Rebel against orders even at risk"
- Forced: "Invoke your masters' name to get what you want" / "Claim you're doing it for your masters when it's actually for your own ends"
- Forced: "Rescue an NPC from trouble you caused" / "Involve an NPC in your masters' schemes"
- Penitent: "Bring bodily harm to those who stand against your order" / "De-escalate a situation that would have led to bloodshed"
- Penitent: "Perform an act in service to your order" / "Do something your order would frown upon"
- Heartsong: "Allow something dangerous of the Heart to live when you could have killed it" / implied destruction beats

This is the most important structural feature of a calling's minor tier. Without tension pairs, the calling becomes a performance of a type rather than a story about a person in conflict.

### Mechanically Specific Beats
Some minor beats reference specific dice values or game resources:
- Forced: "Send a requested resource (D8 or higher) back to your master rather than using it yourself" — resource value threshold
- Forced: "Do a favour for someone to gain leverage over them" — leverage mechanic implied
- Enlightenment: "Mark D8 stress to an NPC bond and describe what happens" — explicit stress value
- Enlightenment: "Sell or sacrifice a D8 or higher resource to secure a secret" — resource value threshold
- Enlightenment: "Reach a landmark on tier 3 of the Heart" — location tier reference

These specific references are used sparingly — typically 1–3 per calling. They ground beats in the system's actual economy without making the whole list feel mechanical.

### The Heartsong Ritual Beat
Heartsong has one beat that creates an ongoing in-play constraint: *"Following a long ritual, name The Heart. Only refer to it by this name from now on."* This beat is unique across all five callings — it is the only minor beat that changes how the player participates in the fiction permanently, rather than describing a single event. No other calling has anything equivalent.

### Core Ability Reference Beats
Forced is the only calling with an explicit beat referencing its core ability by name: *"As a result of using the COLLATERAL ability, an NPC is taken out of action or a PC suffers fallout."* Other callings do not call out their abilities by name in beats. The COLLATERAL reference makes sense because COLLATERAL is the only core ability whose side effects (stress landing on an ally) are themselves worth marking as a story beat.

## 6. Major Beats

### Count
All five callings have exactly **10 major beats**. This is completely consistent — not a range, not an average. Ten.

### Fallout Beats
- Forced: "Take Major Fortune fallout"
- Enlightenment: "Take Major Mind fallout", "Take Major Supplies fallout"
- Adventure: No major fallout beat in major tier (it was moved to minor beats)
- Penitent: "Take Major Blood fallout"
- Heartsong: "Take Major Echo fallout"

As with minor beats, Enlightenment has two fallout tracks. Adventure has none, because it placed both major fallout beats in the minor tier. Every other calling has exactly one.

### Irreversible Acts
Every major beat set includes at least one beat describing an action that cannot be undone:
- Forced: "An important or beloved NPC dies after you bring them into your master's schemes"
- Forced: "Perform a truly reprehensible act on behalf of your masters"
- Enlightenment: "Kill someone who is trying to stop you from claiming knowledge"
- Enlightenment: "Destroy a haunt to learn more about your task"
- Penitent: "Betray someone who really trusts you"
- Penitent: "Eliminate or completely undermine a faction that is opposed to your order"
- Heartsong: "Sacrifice someone important to the Heart"
- Heartsong: "Destroy a haven, returning the land to the Heart"
- Adventure: "Have a landmark or connection named after you" (irreversible in the positive sense — permanent mark on the world)

### Escalation from Minor Beats
Major beats frequently escalate minor beats by raising the stakes of the same action:
- Minor: "Forge a friendship or romantic relationship with an NPC" → Major: "An important or beloved NPC dies after you bring them into your master's schemes" (Forced)
- Minor: "Sell or sacrifice a D8 or higher resource" → Major: "Sell or sacrifice a D12 resource to secure a secret" (Enlightenment — same beat, higher resource tier)
- Minor: "Establish a connection" → Major: "Connect three havens to one another with permanent paths" (Adventure — same action at faction scale)
- Minor: "Rescue someone from peril" → Major: "Save a haven from destruction or doom" (Adventure — same action at haven scale)

This minor→major escalation is one of the primary tools for building calling coherence across the tiers.

### Beats with Explicit Mechanical Modifiers
Two callings include beats in the major tier with explicit mechanical effects written into the beat text itself — these are not just descriptions of fiction but actual rule interventions:

- Forced: *"Complete a major objective for your master – something taking at least two sessions to achieve."* This is the only beat across all five callings that specifies a minimum session duration.
- Enlightenment: *"Explore a mysterious tower whilst on a delve (this increases the difficulty of the delve by D12)."* This is the only beat that adds a mechanical modifier to another game activity (the delve system).

Both are in major beats, not minor. Major beats can carry more mechanical weight than minor beats.

### PC Involvement
Major beats do not directly reference other PCs as frequently as character creation questions do, but several include language that pulls other players in:
- Forced: "Aid another person, who you know and hate, that is also in the employ of your masters"
- Forced: "Thanks to your actions, a haven is pitched into deadly chaos" (affects everyone)
- Enlightenment: "Attract a protégé who's read your work" (introduces NPC but is a PC-adjacent move)
- Heartsong: "One of your bonds takes Critical fallout thanks to your actions"

Only Heartsong explicitly places fallout onto another character's bond as a major beat.

## 7. Zenith Beats

### Count
All five callings have exactly **2 zenith beats**. This is the most consistent number in the entire structure — not once is there a third option or a single option.

### Structure: Fulfillment vs. Inversion
Every pair follows the same pattern: one beat where the calling reaches its intended end, one beat where it reaches the opposite end. The two options always share a premise — usually the same event or moment — but diverge in what the character does with it or what it costs.

| Calling | Fulfillment | Inversion |
|---|---|---|
| Forced | End the control your master has over you | Take bloody revenge on your master |
| Enlightenment | Find the secret and use it to solve your impossible task | Find the secret and destroy it so no one else can know of it |
| Adventure | Reach Tier 4 of the Heart | Lead a haven to prosperity |
| Penitent | Be truly absolved of your sins by a higher power | Betray your order — intentionally, this time |
| Heartsong | Become one with the Heart, and bind your essence to it | Break the hold that the Heart has over you, ripping it from your body and spirit |

Notable: Adventure's pair is the least like a traditional inversion — "Reach Tier 4" vs. "Lead a haven" are both positive outcomes, just in different directions (personal glory vs. settled achievement). This reflects Adventure's nature: the calling is not tragic, so there is no "dark" zenith. Both endings are valid endings for an Adventure character.

### Naming
In the Heart source material, zenith beats are **not named**. They are plain checkbox entries with descriptive text, identical in formatting to all other beats. The ceremonial naming convention ("The Prize", "The Downfall", etc.) does not exist in the reference callings — it appears to have been introduced in the Shackles v5 ideation.

### No "Downfall" Beat
None of the five Heart callings has a separate "Downfall" beat as a distinct named option. The inversion zenith serves the same narrative function, but it is not flagged as a special category. The Downfall convention is a Shackles v5 design addition.

### What Zenith Beats Are Not
In Heart's text, zenith beats carry no special framing beyond their position in the list. They are not described as "retirement triggers" within the calling document itself — that framing exists in the Heart rulebook, not in calling design. The calling text simply lists them as beats like any other.

## 8. Calling Equipment

### Format
The lead-in is always: *"Roll 1d10 on the following table or choose to determine what you carry with you to mark your calling:"*

Always exactly 10 entries numbered 1–10.

### Item Categories
Items fall into four types, and every calling uses all four:

**Physical proof of the calling's condition** — objects that make the calling visible on the character's body or in their belongings. These are the most calling-specific items.
- Forced: Moonsilver collar, Subjugation sliver implanted in your neck, Guild brand on your upper arm, Mark on your chest where your soul used to be
- Penitent: Absolution chains (heavy), Vellum scroll bearing a record of your crimes, Still-itching tattoo depicting your sins
- Heartsong: The word "THEOLOSIAN" growing over your upper chest, Barely-viable homonculus you shamefully coughed up or excised

**Tools of the obsession** — things the character uses in pursuit of their calling.
- Forced: Codebook showing you how and where to make your reports
- Enlightenment: Set of fragile magnifying lenses, Portable alchemy kit in a leather box
- Adventure: Inaccurate map of the Heart, Fake "Abomination-hunting" license
- Heartsong: Ink-blotted dream journal with maps of places you saw when asleep, Steel syringe and opiate powder

**Mundane personal items with implied backstory** — ordinary objects whose significance lies entirely in what they suggest about the character's past. No magical properties. No mechanical effects.
- Forced: Daguerreotype of your son
- Adventure: Mouth organ, Letters from your mum asking when you're going to come home
- Penitent: Book of handwritten, melodramatic poetry, Locket depicting a beautiful one-eyed drow
- Enlightenment: Taxidermied owl (name them)

**Items with dark humour or pointed specificity** — the item that makes the table feel inhabited rather than designed.
- Enlightenment: "Bundled-up red-string-and-pins collage identifying the fools who tried to stop you", "List of people who are Going To Pay, some names crossed out"
- Adventure: "Unpunched Vermissian ticket used as a good-luck charm", "Expensive kohl eyeliner and pocket-mirror"
- Heartsong: "Greenish candles that help you sleep a dreamless sleep (sometimes)"
- Penitent: "Brand identifying you as one of the secretive knights of the Covenant of the Fallen Tower"

### Naming Instructions
Two items across all five callings instruct the player to name something:
- Enlightenment: "Taxidermied owl (name them)"
- Heartsong: "Barely-viable homonculus you shamefully coughed up or excised (name it)"

Both are in callings that deal with obsessive, slightly unhinged characters. The naming instruction creates immediate attachment without requiring backstory writing.

## 9. Calling-Specific Outliers

Each of the five callings breaks a pattern that the other four follow, which reveals where deliberate design exceptions occur:

**Forced** — Has 5 character creation questions instead of 4. The additional question establishes a PC bond in a calling whose other four questions are entirely about the antagonist. The exception compensates for a structural gap: without it, Forced would be the only calling with zero PC bond questions.

**Enlightenment** — Has 2 dominant fallout tracks (Mind and Supplies) where every other calling has 1. This reflects the dual cost of its obsession: pursuing forbidden knowledge costs both sanity and material resources simultaneously.

**Adventure** — Places Major fallout beats (Major Blood, Major Fortune) in the minor tier rather than the major tier. No other calling does this. It also has no major fallout beats in the major tier as a result. This is a deliberate signal about the calling's identity: Adventure burns bright, fast, and catastrophically even in routine moments.

**Heartsong** — The largest minor beat set (23) and the only calling with a constraint-creating beat ("name The Heart and refer to it only by this name from now on"). It is also the only calling whose core ability uses "once per situation" rather than "once per session" for its active component.

**Penitent** — The most internally focused calling. Its questions and beats are almost entirely about the character's own guilt, order, and relationship to redemption. It has the fewest beats that reference the wider world (haven politics, faction power dynamics) and the most that reference acts of service, repair, and penance.

## 10. What the Heart Callings Do Not Include

The following conventions appear in the Shackles v5 callings but are absent from all five Heart reference callings:

- **Named zenith beats.** Heart zeniths are plain checkboxes. The named format ("The Prize", "The Reckoning") is a Shackles addition.
- **A "Downfall" beat category.** Heart has an inversion zenith but does not give it special status or a separate label.
- **Distinctions & Overlaps section.** Heart callings have no comparative section explaining how the calling differs from adjacent ones.
- **An introductory framing paragraph about the calling's thematic territory.** The Shackles callings open with an extended prose section before the questions. Heart callings open only with the short italic flavour text.
- **Domain suggestions.** The Shackles Cursed Tides calling includes a sidebar suggesting the Arcane domain as a complement. Heart callings contain no domain or skill suggestions in the calling text itself.
- **Shared calling.** Heart has no equivalent to Piracy as a universal shared calling.
