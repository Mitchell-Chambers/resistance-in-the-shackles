# Calling Design Guide

This document codifies patterns extracted from the Heart: The City Beneath reference callings and the v5 Shackles calling set. Use it as a checklist and reference when designing new personal callings for Resistance in the Shackles.

---

## 1. What a Calling Is (and Is Not)

A calling is not a class, a background, or a profession. It is the obsession — the wound that has organised itself into a direction. Where a class answers "what can you do?", a calling answers "what can you not stop wanting?".

Two characters with the same class can have entirely different callings. The same calling can be played very differently. The calling is the story the character is in; the class is the toolbox they use to survive it.

**The central design principle:** every calling describes a condition that cannot end quietly. The beats are not tasks to complete — they are the texture of a life organised around a compulsion. The zenith beats are the two or three ways that life resolves.

---

## 2. Structural Overview

Every personal calling is built from the same skeleton, in this order:

1. **Name** — a single evocative word
2. **Flavour subtitle** — one italic phrase naming the calling's core emotional territory
3. **Flavour paragraph(s)** — 1–3 paragraphs establishing who this person is and what drives them. This is not a mechanical description. Write it as if addressing the player.
4. **Core Ability** — bold header, mechanical rule
5. **Character Questions** — 4 questions
6. **Minor Beats** — ~18–22 checkbox entries
7. **Major Beats** — ~8–10 checkbox entries
8. **Zenith Beats** — exactly 2–3 named options (always including a Downfall)
9. **Distinctions & Overlaps** — 2–4 comparisons to adjacent callings
10. **What You Carry** — d10 object table

---

## 3. The Thematic Architecture

Every calling has a three-act dramatic structure embedded in its beat tiers:

- **Minor beats** are the habit and texture of living this way. They cover things the character does often, the way this obsession makes itself visible in ordinary play.
- **Major beats** are the turning points — the moments of irreversible escalation, significant loss, or deepened commitment. They are harder to hit and carry real consequences.
- **Zenith beats** are the endings. They do not represent "winning" or "losing" — they represent the story completing.

The calling should also name a **dominant stress track** — the resistance that most often takes the toll of this life. That track should appear in both a minor and a major fallout beat, and it should feel thematically correct. A character defined by physical recklessness takes Blood fallout. A character whose obsession attracts supernatural attention takes Tide fallout. A character defined by social power and legend takes Reputation fallout.

---

## 4. The Calling Name

One word. It names the condition, not the character type. The name should describe what the character *has*, not what they *do*.

**Good:** Hunger, Vendetta, Fellowship, Monument, Legacy, Cursed Tides, Freedom

**Avoid:** names that describe a class role (Corsair, Duelist, Sorcerer), names that describe a class's mechanical identity, or names so broad they could apply to any pirate.

The flavour subtitle below the name should sharpen the emotional core in a single phrase — it is the thing the name points at, made explicit.

---

## 5. Core Ability

The core ability is the mechanical expression of the calling's emotional truth. It should make the *feeling* of the calling legible in play.

### Design Rules

- **One sentence of flavour, then the rule.** The flavour is in italics or a brief lead-in; the mechanical text is clean and direct.
- **Once per session or always-on — not both.** Most core abilities are one of:
  - A *once per session* active effect (declare a fact, remove stress, guarantee a result)
  - A *passive* effect that is always true (cannot be coerced, gain a bonus when X happens)
- **Simple.** The core ability should be explainable in under thirty words. If it requires sub-clauses, it is doing too much.
- **Connected to the fiction.** The ability should not be a generic bonus dressed in thematic language. It should do something that only makes sense for this specific calling.

### Examples by Structure

| Structure | Example |
|---|---|
| Once per session, declare a fact | *Hunger:* "Once per session, you may declare that you know a rumour about the location or nature of something you are hunting. The GM must confirm whether it is true or false." |
| Always-on immunity | *Freedom:* "You cannot be coerced into a course of action you have explicitly refused. When an NPC attempts to threaten, blackmail, or magically compel you, you automatically succeed on resisting." |
| Once per session, reactive stress removal | *Fellowship:* "Once per session, when a fellow PC or named NPC crewmember takes fallout of any kind, you may immediately remove one stress from any of your own resistances." |
| Once per session, ask the GM a question | *Cursed Tides / Monument:* "Once per session, you may ask the GM [something specific]. The GM must answer honestly." |

The "ask the GM and they must answer honestly" structure is particularly powerful for callings built around knowledge, intelligence, or supernatural attunement. Use it when the calling's identity is about *knowing* rather than *doing*.

## 6. Character Questions

Every calling has exactly **4 character questions**. These are asked at the table during character creation.

### Design Rules

- **At least 2 questions should reference other PCs.** Questions that tie characters together before the campaign starts are structural investment in the calling playing out well.
- **At least 1 question should ground the motivation.** "What drove you to this?" / "What happened that you can't walk away from?" / "What do you want that you can't name to yourself?"
- **At least 1 question should establish something already visible in the world** — a consequence, a mark, a person, a debt — that will resurface in play. The beats will cash these in.
- **Questions should not ask for information that appears nowhere else in the calling.** Every answer should connect to at least one beat.

### Question Structures That Work

- *What [event] led you to [this condition]?*
- *Who [relationship] has [history] with you — and how are they involved?*
- *What does [the thing you are pursuing] look like to you in your most honest moments?*
- *What have you [already given up / already done] and does it still seem like a fair trade?*
- *What do you [do / avoid / carry] that reveals [the truth of your condition]?*

---

## 7. Minor Beats

### Count

Aim for **18–22 minor beats**. Fewer than 18 gives players too few options to work with; more than 22 becomes unwieldy.

### Required Inclusions

Every minor beat set must include at least one of each of the following:

- **Take Minor [Dominant Track] fallout** — the calling's toll, named directly.
- **A beat that actively expresses the calling's core drive** — doing the thing the calling makes you do.
- **A beat that contradicts or complicates the calling's drive** — running from it, failing it, refusing it, or being forced to choose against it. This tension beat is what makes the calling interesting rather than just a performance of a type.
- **An NPC relationship beat** — forge a bond, betray one, lose one, acquire one who enables the obsession.
- **A revelation beat** — describe or share something about the calling's backstory, the thing that made you this way.
- **A consequence beat** — get in trouble, attract attention, put others at risk because of the obsession.

### Beat Construction

Beats should be written as situations the character *moves toward* — things they choose or that happen around them as a result of who they are. Avoid beats that are only about rolling dice or mechanical outcomes.

**Strong minor beat:** *"Let the hunger lead you into a clearly dangerous situation without a plan."*

**Weak minor beat:** *"Succeed at a difficult Hunt roll."*

Beats are not instructions. They describe a moment in fiction, not an action to perform. The player selects beats at the end of a session to indicate what they want the coming sessions to address.

### Tension Pairs

The most effective callings have at least one **tension pair** — two beats that describe opposite responses to the same pressure:

- Follow orders even at risk / Rebel against orders even at risk *(Forced)*
- Refuse a direct order from someone you respect / Pass up something rather than work under someone's authority *(Freedom)*
- Commit an act of deliberate construction / Refuse to back down because retreat would undo something built *(Monument)*

The tension pair is what prevents a calling from being a simple fantasy. The beats say: this is what you do *and* this is what it costs you to keep doing it.

---

## 8. Major Beats

### Count

Aim for **8–10 major beats**. They require two or more sessions to achieve and represent genuine turning points.

### Required Inclusions

Every major beat set must include:

- **Take Major [Dominant Track] fallout** — the higher-tier cost.
- **An irreversible act** — destroy, kill, betray, or lose something that cannot be simply restored.
- **A faction or institution beat** — gain authority within, make an enemy of, formally come to the attention of, or be formally excluded from, a named faction (Free Captains, Chelish Navy, Aspis Consortium, a specific fleet, etc.).
- **A beat that involves another PC** — major beats should occasionally pull other players into the story.
- **A partial victory or qualified failure** — at least one beat that is about "this happened, but not cleanly." Callings should not only have beats about triumph or disaster.

### Escalation

Major beats are minor beats with consequences that last. Many major beats work by taking a minor beat's core action and making it permanent, irreversible, or costly:

- Minor: *"Put another PC at genuine risk because of your obsession."*
- Major: *"Cause a PC or significant NPC genuine, lasting harm through your single-minded pursuit."*

When designing major beats, ask: what does this calling look like when the brakes fail?

---

## 9. Zenith Beats

Every calling has **2–3 zenith beats**. The last one is always the **Downfall** (see section 10).

### Design Rule: Binary Resolution

Zenith beats represent the two or three ways this calling's story can end. They should not be stages — a player selects one, not all of them in sequence.

The standard structure is **fulfillment vs. inversion**: achieve the thing you came for / burn it, betray it, or be destroyed by it. With a third option, the middle path often involves transformation or handoff — becoming something new, or passing the calling's weight to someone else.

### Naming Zenith Beats

Zenith beats are named. The name should be short, evocative, and standalone — a reader who doesn't know the calling should still feel its weight from the name alone.

Good: *The Prize. The Reckoning. The Ancestor. The Gift. The Fire. The Inheritance.*

Avoid: *Complete your goal. Face the consequences.* (Too generic — could be any calling.)

### What Makes a Good Zenith

- **Enormously significant in scope** — reaching somewhere no chart shows, bringing down an institution, binding your essence to something, giving everything for the crew.
- **Narratively irreversible** — the character cannot return to who they were.
- **Thematically specific** — it should only make sense for *this* calling. A Zenith beat from Cursed Tides would not make sense for Fellowship.
- **It signals the end** — selecting a zenith begins the final one or two sessions of the character's arc. The player knows this when they choose it.

---

## 10. The Downfall Beat

Every calling's final zenith is always the Downfall.

The Downfall is the ending where the obsession wins — not in the triumphant sense, but in the sense that the specific collapse the calling always pointed toward finally arrives.

### Design Rules

- It is **never required**. Players select it when they are ready for their character's story to end in the way their calling always threatened.
- It should name the **specific collapse** this calling produces. Not "die" — but *how* this kind of life ends this kind of way.
- It should carry its own weight as an ending. The Downfall is not a failure state. It is, in its own way, a form of completion.

### Good Downfall Beats

> ***The Downfall.*** Die for it, or lose everything chasing it, and in the final moment decide whether the wanting was ever really about the thing at all. *(Hunger)*

> ***The Downfall.*** Realise the vendetta has become you — and let it finish what it started, or burn it out and find out who is left in the ash. *(Vendetta)*

> ***The Downfall.*** Lose them — all of them, or the ones that mattered most — and discover whether there is anything left of you when the thing that made you whole is gone. *(Fellowship)*

The Downfall should feel like it could only belong to this calling. If you could swap the Downfall text between two callings and it still made sense, at least one of them needs to be more specific.

---

## 11. Distinctions & Overlaps

Every calling should include a **Distinctions & Overlaps** section after the zenith beats. This section exists to help players choose between callings that address similar territory.

### How Many

Write **2–4 comparisons**. Every calling should compare itself to its nearest neighbours — the callings that a player might reasonably consider if they were drawn to this one.

### Structure

Each comparison entry has two parts:
1. **Name both callings** in bold at the top.
2. **Identify the specific axis of difference** — the one question that, when answered, tells a player which calling is right for them.

The test: a player who has read both callings and is still uncertain should read the comparison and immediately know which one fits their character.

### Good Distinctions Identify a Decision Point

> **VENDETTA vs. HUNGER** — *Ask the player: if your target surrendered the thing they took from you but walked away free, would that be enough? If yes, the calling is Hunger. If the answer is no — if what you need is to look them in the eye — the calling is Vendetta.*

> **MONUMENT vs. FELLOWSHIP** — *Monument treats relationships as structural — bricks in a larger construction. Fellowship treats them as intrinsic — the point, not the means. A Monument character will sacrifice a friendship if the structure demands it. A Fellowship character will sacrifice the structure before they sacrifice the friendship.*

## 12. What You Carry

Every calling ends with a table of ten objects. Roll 1d10 or choose.

### Design Rules

- **Objects are not equipment.** They do not grant bonuses or occupy inventory slots. They are evidence — of the life the character was living before the campaign began, of the obsession that brought them here.
- **Mix the mundane and the strange.** Not every item should be eerie or supernatural. A list of names, a letter that was never sent, a gun never fired at anyone else — these are as resonant as a piece of enchanted coral.
- **2–3 items should function as visible proof of the calling.** Something another character could see and recognise as belonging to this kind of person.
- **1–2 items with dark humour or specificity.** The detail that makes the table feel like someone lived it: *"Letters from your mum asking when you're going to come home."* *"A broadsheet carrying a story about you — the details are wrong in three specific ways, none of which you have corrected."*
- **Items should invite questions.** Players do not need to explain every item at character creation. The best items are the ones that will come up in play.

## 13. Stress Track Reference

When assigning fallout beats, use the correct current track names. The calling's **dominant track** should appear in both a minor and a major fallout beat.

| Track | Represents | Natural Dominant Track For |
|---|---|---|
| **Blood** | Physical damage — wounds, exhaustion, disease, the sea's violence on bodies | Vendetta, Penitent, physical recklessness |
| **Tide** | Supernatural contamination — old things getting in, curse exposure, deep-water attunement | Cursed Tides, any calling with strong supernatural involvement |
| **Reputation** | Social damage — your name, your legend, the fear and respect you command | Monument, Legacy, any calling built around standing |
| **Luck** | Fated misfortune — the universe collecting on audacious acts and pirate debt | Hunger, Adventure, recklessness-driven callings |
| **Gold** | Material damage — prize losses, debt, the cost of staying free | Hunger, any calling with strong material stakes |

A calling may have fallout beats from **two tracks** if both fit the thematic territory. A calling defined by physical recklessness and social ambition might take both Blood and Reputation fallout. However, one track should clearly dominate — the one that fires most often across minor and major beats.

**Note on old track names:** Earlier draft callings used Nerve (→ Blood or Luck), Loot (→ Gold), and Hex (→ Tide). These names are retired. Use the current five-track names above.

## 14. Shackles-Specific Conventions

When translating or building callings for the Shackles, replace Heart-specific terminology with the following:

| Heart Term | Shackles Equivalent |
|---|---|
| The Heart | The Sea, the Eye of Abendego, Besmara's domain, the deep |
| Haven | Port, anchorage, free harbour, home ship |
| Tier 3 of the Heart | The open ocean, uncharted waters, the Eye itself, drowned ruins |
| Vermissian Station | Shackles waypoint, smugglers' route, secret anchorage |
| Echo resistance | Tide resistance |
| Supplies resistance | Gold resistance |
| Fortune resistance | Luck resistance |
| City Above / City Beneath | The mainland / the Shackles |
| The Hounds (Heart faction) | Chelish Navy, Aspis Consortium, bounty hunters |
| Ivory Row / Spire factions | Free Captains, Pirate Council, port authorities, colonial powers |

When referencing factions, be specific. "A hostile power" is weaker than "the Chelish Navy, the Aspis Consortium, or a rival Free Captain." Named institutions make beats feel grounded.

## 15. The Shared Calling (Piracy)

Every character holds two callings simultaneously: Piracy (shared) and their personal calling. At the end of each session, the player chooses two active beats from either pool — the pools are combined and there is no hierarchy between them.

### Design Implications for Personal Callings

- **Do not put the life of piracy into personal callings.** Beats about storms, prizes, ports, and the Code belong in Piracy. Personal calling beats should be about the specific obsession — the thing that makes *this* character different from any other pirate.
- **Personal callings should create tension with the shared life.** The best personal calling beats are the ones that pull the character away from the obvious pirate action, or that complicate a standard pirate situation with a layer of personal stakes.
- **The Piracy calling handles the economics.** Gold fallout beats from storms, lost prizes, and port debts live in Piracy. Personal callings only include Gold fallout if the *specific obsession* has material consequences.

---

## 16. A Note on Beat Framing

Beats are chosen by players at the end of sessions, not during play. When a player selects a beat, they are acting as the director of their character's story — indicating to the GM what they want the next session to address.

This means:

- Beats that impose costs on the character (fallout, betrayal, loss) are **invitations**, not punishments.
- Beats do not require GM setup to "earn." The player selects them because they want to see the scene; the GM weaves it in.
- **A character does not know they have beats.** The beat is a meta-tool for the player, not a motivation the character is aware of.

When writing beats, write them as scenes and situations — not as tasks. The player should read a beat and immediately picture the kind of moment it describes.

## 17. Quick Design Checklist

### Calling Identity
- [ ] Name is a single evocative word describing a condition, not a class role
- [ ] Flavour subtitle names the emotional territory
- [ ] Flavour paragraphs establish *why* this person cannot stop, not just *what* they do
- [ ] Dominant stress track identified

### Core Ability
- [ ] Once per session or passive — not both
- [ ] Explainable in under thirty words
- [ ] Would only make sense for this specific calling

### Character Questions
- [ ] Exactly 4 questions
- [ ] At least 2 reference other PCs
- [ ] At least 1 grounds the motivation
- [ ] At least 1 establishes something already visible in the world
- [ ] Every answer connects to at least one beat

### Minor Beats (~18–22 entries)
- [ ] Take Minor [Dominant Track] fallout included
- [ ] Active expression of the calling's drive included
- [ ] Tension beat (contradicts or complicates the drive) included
- [ ] NPC relationship beat included
- [ ] Revelation beat (sharing the backstory) included
- [ ] Consequence beat (put others at risk) included
- [ ] At least one tension pair (opposite responses to the same pressure)

### Major Beats (~8–10 entries)
- [ ] Take Major [Dominant Track] fallout included
- [ ] At least one irreversible act
- [ ] At least one faction or institution beat
- [ ] At least one beat involving another PC
- [ ] At least one partial victory or qualified failure

### Zenith Beats (2–3 entries)
- [ ] Fulfillment option included
- [ ] Inversion or collapse option included
- [ ] Downfall beat included and listed last
- [ ] Each zenith is named
- [ ] Each name is specific enough to belong only to this calling
- [ ] Downfall names the *specific* collapse this calling produces

### Distinctions & Overlaps
- [ ] 2–4 comparisons to adjacent callings
- [ ] Each comparison identifies a decision point, not just a difference
- [ ] The comparison would help an undecided player choose

### What You Carry (d10 table)
- [ ] 10 entries
- [ ] Mix of mundane personal items and thematically strange objects
- [ ] 2–3 items function as visible proof of the calling
- [ ] 1–2 items carry dark humour or specific human detail
- [ ] Items invite questions rather than explain themselves
