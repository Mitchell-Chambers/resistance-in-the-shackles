# Ancestry Design Guide
*Resistance in the Shackles — a reference for creating new ancestries compatible with the Heart: The City Beneath system*

---

## What Is an Ancestry?

An ancestry is a purely narrative construct. It tells the table who your character is in the fiction of the Shackles — where they came from, how the world has treated them, and what ghosts they carry into a life of piracy. It does not tell the table what they can do. There are no stat bonuses, no mechanical abilities, no resistance modifiers attached to an ancestry. That work belongs entirely to your Class and Calling.

This is an intentional design choice inherited from Heart: The City Beneath. The sea treats everyone equally. The storm doesn't care if you're a half-orc or a Chelish noble. What matters is who you are before the dice hit the table — the grudges, the loyalties, the reason you're standing on a deck at the edge of the world.

> **An ancestry answers: Who are you? Where do you come from? Why are you here? It never answers: What can you do?**

---

## Anatomy of an Ancestry

Every ancestry contains exactly four components, always in the same order. Deviating from this structure creates inconsistency at the table and signals to players that one ancestry is more mechanically significant than another — which it should never be.

### 1. The Lore Block

Four to six sentences of plain prose. No bullet points, no sub-headers. The lore block does four things in sequence:

1. **Physical description.** One or two sentences establishing what this ancestry looks like. Keep it evocative but not exhaustive — players will fill in the details.
2. **Place in the world.** How does this ancestry relate to the Shackles' power structures? Are they the colonised or the colonisers? Outlaws by law or by choice? Newcomers buying their way in?
3. **The pull of the sea.** Why do people of this ancestry end up on a pirate ship? What does a life of freedom on the water mean specifically to them?
4. **A cultural or spiritual note.** One sentence about faith, custom, or worldview. This gives players immediate texture and seeds roleplay.

> *Design note: The lore block should always end on a forward beat — a reason to be here, now, on this ship. Ancestries that end on a purely historical or tragic note feel static. Give them momentum.*

---

### 2. Three Background Questions

When creating a character, the player answers one of three questions. These are not open-ended prompts; they are structured scenario seeds. Each one names specific places, factions, or events from the Shackles — engaging with the world is mandatory, not optional.

#### Question Architecture

Every question follows a two-part structure: a framing sentence that establishes the scenario, followed by a follow-up question that the player must answer out loud. The follow-up is what anchors the character to the fiction.

> *Good structure:* "You served on a Chelish privateer vessel before the Free Captains captured your ship. What did you do to earn their trust instead of a quick death?"
>
> *Weak structure:* "Describe your character's life before becoming a pirate."

#### The Three Questions Cover Different Vectors

The three questions should not be variations on the same theme. Together, they must triangulate the range of reasons someone of that ancestry joins a pirate crew. A reliable framework:

- **Origin above.** The character comes from somewhere specific outside the Shackles and arrived by circumstance (fleeing, seeking, sold, hired).
- **Origin in the Shackles.** The character was born or raised in the islands. Their relationship to piracy is intimate and complicated.
- **Purpose or transformation.** The character is defined less by where they came from and more by what they're looking for or what they've left behind.

#### Name the World

Every question should contain at least one proper noun: a port, a faction, a deity, a ship name, a historical event. Vague questions are weak questions.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| a colonial power | a Chelish man-o-war out of Corentyn |
| a religious institution | a Rahadoumi ship of the Pure Legion |
| your homeland | the kelp-forests of the Sodden Lands |
| a pirate lord | Tessa Fairwind of Quent |
| a god | Besmara, the Pirate Queen |

---

### 3. Example Names

A list of four to six names typical of that ancestry, with a one-sentence note on naming conventions if the culture has a distinctive pattern. The note does as much work as the names themselves — it tells players how names function socially (are they earned? given at birth? translated for outsiders?).

> *Example: Example Garundi names: Adaeze, Kofi, Ngozi, Seun, Yetunde. Many Garundi sailors take a ship-name in addition to their birth name; this is the name used among crew.*

---

### 4. The Starting Trinkets Table

A d20 table of keepsakes and curiosities. Players roll twice and keep both items. The table is the most visible expression of an ancestry's culture — it should feel like a small museum of a people's life.

#### What the Table Must Do

- **Reflect the culture specifically.** Items that could appear on any table are wasted slots. A vial of rare indigo dye, a shark-tooth charm from a specific island, a letter of marque with the wrong name — these are ancestry items. A dagger is not.
- **Mix the mundane and the strange.** Some items should be familiar objects with emotional weight (a parent's compass, a child's drawing of a ship). Some should be weird or darkly funny. This tonal range is essential.
- **Include naming prompts.** At least two or three items should have a "(name them)" prompt attached: a familiar, a pet, a beloved weapon. These create immediate attachment.
- **Touch the political.** One or two items should reflect the ancestry's relationship to power: a wanted notice, stolen documents, a brand or scar referenced obliquely, a forged seal.
- **Seed backstory hooks.** The best items imply a story without telling it. "A letter you haven't opened yet" is better than "a letter from home."

#### Table Composition Guidelines

| Category | Recommended Slots (of 20) |
|---|---|
| Personal / emotional items | 4–5 |
| Cultural / religious artifacts | 3–4 |
| Political / factional items | 2–3 |
| Strange / humorous items | 3–4 |
| Living creatures with names | 1–2 |
| Items with implied mechanical use | 1–2 |
| Open-ended / story-hook items | 2–3 |

> *On implied mechanical items: One or two items may hint at mechanical use (a small blade, a pouch of alchemical reagents), but they must never include stat blocks or tags. They are interesting objects first. If a character's class or calling makes them relevant, the GM and player can develop that together.*

## The Core Design Rule

**Ancestries have no mechanics.**

This cannot be overstated. Heart: The City Beneath made this choice deliberately and it defines the feel of play. When a class gives you an ability, it's because of what you've trained to do. When a calling shapes your arc, it's because of what you want. Neither of those things is determined by birth.

Introducing mechanical differentiation to ancestries — even minor, "flavour" bonuses — creates pressure to optimise ancestry choice for class synergy. It turns a question about who you are into a question about what combo is strongest. Resist this at every stage of design.

> *If you find yourself writing an ancestry and thinking "this feels like it should give a bonus to…" — stop. That instinct belongs in a Class ability or a Calling beat, not here.*

## Social Positioning

Every ancestry in the Shackles exists in relationship to power. The Shackles are a contested region: colonial powers (Cheliax, Rahadoum, the Aspis Consortium) want them controlled; the Free Captains want them free; various indigenous peoples have been there far longer than any of them. An ancestry that doesn't engage with this tension is an ancestry that doesn't belong in this setting.

When designing a new ancestry, the first question to answer is not physical appearance. It's this:

> **Where does this ancestry stand in the Shackles' web of power, and what does that mean for someone who ends up on a pirate ship?**

Some ancestries will be people who have had piracy forced on them by colonial dispossession. Some will be willing participants drawn by freedom. Some will be former agents of oppressive powers in complicated exile. Some will be children of the Shackles born into this life. All are valid. None should be morally flattened.

| Position | Design Implications |
|---|---|
| Colonised / dispossessed | Questions should address what was lost and what the sea offers instead. Trinkets carry cultural memory. |
| Colonial / privileged origin | Questions should address the moment of rupture — why they left, what they gave up. Trinkets carry markers of status they can't use anymore. |
| Stateless / outlawed | Questions should address identity outside the law. What does loyalty mean without a nation? |
| Born to the Shackles | Questions address what it means to have no "before." The sea is home, not escape. |
| Foreign visitor / exile | Questions address why they're here rather than there. What do they miss? What are they hiding? |

## Anchoring to the Shackles

Heart's ancestries are anchored to Spire and the Heart. Every question names a location, faction, or institution from that world. Our ancestries must do the same with the Shackles.

### Locations

- **Port Peril** — seat of the Hurricane King; the closest thing to a capital in the Shackles
- **Quent** — largest city in the Shackles; Tessa Fairwind's stronghold
- **Bloodcove** — Aspis Consortium port; legitimate commerce and deep corruption
- **Corentyn** — Chelish city; origin point for many colonial pressures
- **The Sodden Lands** — flooded, ruined territory north of the Shackles
- **The Eye of Abendego** — permanent hurricane in the north; sacred, terrifying, impassable

### Factions & Powers

- **The Free Captains** — loose confederation of pirate lords; the governing fiction of the Shackles
- **The Hurricane King** — nominal ruler of the Shackles; position held by whoever is powerful enough to keep it
- **Cheliax** — devil-allied empire; primary colonial aggressor in the region
- **The Aspis Consortium** — mercantile organisation; amoral, ubiquitous, dangerous
- **The Rahadoumi Pure Legion** — godless republic's military; hostile to divine magic and its practitioners

### Deities

- **Besmara** — Pirate Queen; goddess of piracy, strife, and sea monsters; most popular deity in the Shackles
- **Gozreh** — god/goddess of wind and waves; respected by sailors across all factions
- **Cayden Cailean** — Accidental God; patron of freedom, ale, and bravery
- **Norgorber** — god of secrets and poison; quietly popular among those who survive by cunning

## Connecting to the Thematic Core

This system uses the Resistance mechanics from Heart to tell a specific story: the inevitable fate of pirates. Freedom and light-heartedness are real, but they exist in the shadow of a reckoning that is always coming. Every ancestry should carry some version of this tension in its lore and its questions.

This doesn't mean every ancestry is grim. It means every ancestry understands that the life they've chosen or been given has a cost. The trinkets table is often where this lands most naturally: a dead friend's hat, a scar described in indirect language, a debt written on paper you carry but can't pay.

> *Ask of every ancestry:*
> - *What does freedom cost someone of this ancestry?*
> - *What is the thing this ancestry is running from, looking for, or slowly losing?*
> - *How does a pirate's life look from inside this ancestry's history?*

## Design Checklist

Before finalising an ancestry, verify each of the following.

### Lore Block
- [ ] Physical description present (1–2 sentences)
- [ ] Social/political position in the Shackles clearly stated
- [ ] Reason this ancestry ends up at sea or on a pirate crew
- [ ] Cultural or spiritual note included
- [ ] Ends on a forward-facing beat, not a static one

### Background Questions
- [ ] Three questions, covering three distinct vectors of arrival
- [ ] Each question names at least one proper noun from the setting
- [ ] Each question ends with a concrete follow-up the player must answer
- [ ] None of the questions offer a mechanical advantage or imply one

### Example Names
- [ ] Four to six names provided
- [ ] Naming convention note included if the culture has a distinctive pattern

### Trinkets Table
- [ ] 20 items (rolled twice on a d20)
- [ ] At least two naming prompts (name them/her/him)
- [ ] At least two items with political or factional resonance
- [ ] At least two items with personal/emotional weight
- [ ] At least one strange or darkly humorous item
- [ ] No items that function as stat blocks or tagged weapons
- [ ] All proper nouns are consistent with the Shackles setting

### Overall
- [ ] No mechanical bonuses, abilities, or modifiers anywhere in the document
- [ ] Social positioning is clear and morally non-flattened
- [ ] The ancestry could plausibly combine with any Class and any Calling
- [ ] Reading the ancestry makes you want to play that character

---

*Resistance in the Shackles is a game about freedom and its cost. Build ancestries that know both.*
