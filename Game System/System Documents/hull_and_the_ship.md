# Hull & the Ship

*Status: drafted from the decisions of 2026-06-10. The ship is a shared resistance track. Stress never sinks a ship — fallout does. Durations are fiction-scoped throughout (pure Heart: no rounds, no exchanges).*

---

## THE HULL TRACK

The party's vessel has a single resistance track, **Hull**, held collectively by the crew. It works exactly like a personal resistance:

- It has **protection**, derived from the ship's quality and upgrades (values to be set in the ship-building rules; a sound working vessel starts at Hull protection 1).
- It takes **stress** when things go wrong at sea, reduced by protection as normal.
- When stress lands, the GM **rolls for fallout** against total Hull stress with the usual D12 check.

Anyone aboard can act for the ship; the Hull track belongs to no one and to everyone, like a shared Supplies pool. Heart's grammar all applies: abilities that say "mark stress" can mark Hull where the fiction supports it, Tend rolls can remove it, protection reduces it.

## WHAT RISKS HULL

Stakes-setting decides whether a hazard threatens the ship or the people on her, exactly as the GM establishes any stress type:

- **Hull:** broadsides, reef-strikes, storm seas, fire aboard, rams, things with tentacles and opinions.
- **Blood:** boarding actions, falls from the rigging, splinter wounds, the fight on the deck.
- **Either, GM's call:** a near-miss broadside (Hull, or Blood for the gun crew), running a blockade (Hull, or Luck), grounding at speed.

On journeys, the Hull track is the crew's communal buffer: weather and sea hazards default to Hull rather than personal resistances while the party is aboard — but only the sea's work, not the crew's mistakes. The full split, and the four other places delve rules invert aboard ship, are in **Journeys at Sea** (this folder).

## HULL FALLOUT

**Stress never sinks the ship. Fallout does the talking.**

- **MINOR** — sprung seams (the pumps must be manned; journey actions involving speed are Risky); torn canvas (lose the weather gauge; pursuit and escape rolls are Risky); fouled rigging; cracked spar; the galley fire is out and the crew is cold and surly.
- **MAJOR** — mast down (no flight; the next engagement comes to you); breach at the waterline (the hold floods; cargo and a resource of the GM's choice are lost; sinking begins if unaddressed); fire below (spreading; every action aboard is Risky until contained); rudder gone (the sea decides your heading).
- **CRITICAL** — **the ship is lost**: sinking under you, burning to the waterline, broken on the reef, or taken. As with character death, the GM frames it; unlike character death, the crew's story usually continues — in the boats, in the water, in chains.

GMs should write Hull fallout like character fallout: a concrete problem with a mechanical edge, removable at the right haunt for the right price.

## TENDING THE SHIP

- **Hull stress** is removed like Blood stress: a **Tend+[Domain]** roll aboard the vessel removes D4, increased by equipment (*tar, canvas and copper patching kit — Tend Hull D6*). The Sawbone's THE SAME SAW exists for exactly this.
- **Hull fallout** needs the ship's equivalent of a haunt: a **shipyard, careening beach or graving dock**. These are haunts that list Hull (e.g. *Hagar's yard and careening beach — D6 Hull*), paid for like any haunt — trade, downgrade a resource, or pay in stress (usually Gold, or Blood for labouring it yourselves). Minor Hull fallout can be cleared at a careening cove; Major wants real facilities, which is why the nearest friendly yard matters more to a pirate than the nearest church.
- Equipment being equipment: **no gear removes Tide or Luck**, including the ship's. A cursed keel is not a carpentry problem.

## ENEMY SHIPS

Enemy vessels are **adversaries**, exactly as Heart stats NPCs: one **Resistance** (crew, hull and will to fight, taken together), a protection value, a difficulty, stress dice they inflict, and resources they yield when taken. When an enemy ship's resistance hits 0, it strikes its colours, founders, burns or flees — whatever the players were trying to achieve. A ship you intend to *capture intact* is the same statblock with the stakes declared up front.

> **COLONIAL REVENUE CUTTER** *(adversary)*
> Fast, over-gunned for her size, crewed by men paid just enough to be brave.
> DIFFICULTY: Standard · RESISTANCE: 8 · PROTECTION: 1
> STRESS: D6 (cannon, Hull or Blood per stakes; Ranged)
> RESOURCES: Excise stamps and sealed orders (D6, Colonial); powder and shot (D6, Outlaw)

## TIME AT SEA — NO EXCHANGES

Per the pure-Heart ruling, naval play uses no round structure. Effects are scoped by the fiction: "your next action," "until you next act," "once per situation," "for the remainder of the situation." Repeating harm is expressed the way Heart expresses all situational pressure — **through action results and GM-inflicted consequences**:

- A storm, a running battle or a fire doesn't tick on a clock. While the crew is in it, **any failed or partially successful action to sail, fight or flee marks the listed stress to Hull** — and when the crew stalls, hides or does nothing, the GM inflicts it directly, because the sea does not wait for you to act.
- Example rewrite (Storm Calling): *"Ships caught in the storm mark D8 Hull stress whenever they fail or partially succeed on an action to sail, fight or free themselves within it — and whenever the GM judges the storm simply finds them."*
- Effects that previously lasted "one exchange" last **until someone next acts on them**; effects on "the first exchange" apply to **each participant's first action**.

The full list of class-text lines this rewrites is in `class_errata_pending.md`.
