The Pitch

Blood, Water & Violent Wind

A pirate campaign in the Shackles, using the Resistance system.

You are not heroes; the sea does not care for heroes. You are pirates.

This campaign uses a homebrew system built on the foundation of Heart: The City Beneath's Resistance engine, with its nautical elements drawn from Wildsea. It is not a traditional toybox RPG the way D&D is. It is a game designed to tell a specific kind of story arc: the story of what piracy costs, and whether you believe that price is worth it.

You will have liberty. You will have light-hearted, spectacular, disreputable fun — the boarding actions, the impossible escapes, the nights in port that become legends. That part is real, and the system is built to let you enjoy it without apology.

And then there is the reckoning. The good times are real, and so is what comes after. Luck runs out. The sea keeps its own ledger. The most honest endings available in the Shackles are rarely the quiet ones — and your character will get to choose what theirs is worth.

The rest of this reference tells you how all of that works: the world you sail, the dice you roll, the harm you carry, the obsession that keeps you out here, and the hard-won expertise that has kept you alive so far.

The World

The Shackles

The Shackles is an archipelago of hundreds of islands stretching along the western coast of Garund, a large tropical continent reminiscent of Africa. It lies touching the south of the Eye of Abendego, an immortal hurricane that has not stopped spinning in over a thousand years. Nobody made it. Nobody can stop it. The northern Shackles are its permanent shadow.

The region exists in permanent opposition to civilised power. To the north, Cheliax is the dominant naval force of the Inner Sea — disciplined, aggressive, convinced of its own righteousness and the power of its devil gods. To the south, colonies support trade routes for far-flung empires in Tian Xia and eastern Garund. The Shackles exists, in large part, to rob both — along with everyone else they can find — and to drink well afterwards.

The Free Captains & the Pirate Council

The Shackles has no state. What it has instead is the Free Captains: independent pirate lords who rule from their ships and their islands through reputation, violence, and the fragile web of obligation known as the Pirate Code. The most powerful of these captains sit on the Pirate Council, which meets in Port Peril, the largest city in the Shackles. The Council does not govern so much as manage conflict. It sets prizes, arbitrates disputes, and occasionally decides to make someone's life very short.

Port Peril is the heart of the Shackles — a teeming, dangerous, magnificent city built into a natural harbour, where every vice is available and every transaction has an angle. It is where careers are made and ended, where the Pirate Code is law, and where the smell of salt and smoke never fully leaves the air.

The Eye of Abendego

The Eye is the defining feature of the Shackles. When it arrived a thousand years ago it destroyed the nation of Lirgen, the kingdom of Yamasa, and the empire of Ghol-Gan — and it has not relented since. It is supernaturally powerful; no natural storm sustains itself for a millennium. Scholars of the event believe it is either a manifestation of divine wrath — perhaps the death of a god — or the consequence of a ritual that went catastrophically wrong. Either way, it is not going anywhere.

The Eye shapes Shackles weather, Shackles culture, and Shackles theology. Besmara, the Pirate Queen — goddess of strife, sea monsters, and plunder — is worshipped here more fervently than anywhere else in the world. She does not promise safety. She promises that if you are strong enough, fast enough, and sufficiently disrespectful of consequences, you might deserve what you take.

The Islands & the Deep

The Shackles' islands range from thriving pirate ports to uninhabitable jungle ecosystems to ruins that predate human civilisation. The cyclops empire of Ghol-Gan built its monuments across the entire archipelago before vanishing. Their ruins dot the landscape above water and below, still full of traps, guardians, and things that have been waiting in the dark for a long time. Older things still lurk in the deepest trenches and beneath certain islands that have never been properly settled, for reasons that become obvious quickly.

The sea itself is alive with magic: currents that seem to make navigational decisions; creatures of impossible size; ghost-lights that lead sailors to beautiful deaths; sea witches who trade in storm-debts and claim the ocean as their territory; liches and dragons hiding deep in the jungle from adventurers seeking glory.

This is not a safe sea. But it is a magnificent one.

The Crew

The crew is everything. Not an abstraction, not a resource, not a means to an end — people, specific people, whose names you know and whose faces you could draw from memory in the dark. Pirates talk about freedom like it is a solo act. The best of them know better: the only real freedom most ever feel is the kind they share. Whatever your character's private obsession, the ship and the hands aboard her are the ground you stand on — and, in this game, the thing the sea will most reliably use to hurt you.

How to Play

This game runs on the Resistance engine from Heart: The City Beneath. Play is a conversation: the GM describes the world, you say what your character does, and dice are rolled only when an action has real stakes and an uncertain outcome. Everything below is the machinery beneath that conversation.

The Core Roll

When your character attempts something with potential consequences, you roll a pool of ten-sided dice (D10) and keep the single highest — you never add dice together. You always roll at least one die. Add another for a relevant skill, another for a relevant domain, and one more for a mastery die if a knack applies (to a maximum of four dice). More dice means a better chance of a high result.

Highest die Result
10 Critical success. You get what you wanted, and something extra goes right. Step up your outgoing stress dice.
8–9 Success. You get what you wanted. No stress.
6–7 Success at a cost. You get what you wanted, but you take stress or face a complication.
2–5 Failure. It goes wrong. You take stress; the GM describes what happens.
1 Critical failure. It goes badly wrong. Take double stress.

Difficulty

Most actions are Standard — roll your pool, keep the highest. When circumstances are against you, the action is harder:

  • Risky — remove the highest die from your pool before reading the result.
  • Dangerous — remove the two highest dice.

Good or excellent equipment, a clever plan, or the right preparation can step a Dangerous action down to Risky, or a Risky action down to Standard.

Stress & Fallout

Stress is the Resistance system's currency of consequence. It is not a hit-point bar. It is the accumulation of harm — physical, supernatural, social, fated, material — that erodes a person until something finally breaks.

When you fail or partially succeed, you mark stress to a track. The GM chooses the track based on what is happening in the fiction, not the skill you used, and sets the stress dice by severity:

Dice Severity
D4 Minor trouble
D6 Serious
D8 Genuinely dangerous
D10 Catastrophic

Protection — from class abilities, circumstances, or the Block tag — reduces the number rolled before it is marked.

Each character has five stress tracks: Blood, Tide, Reputation, Luck, and Gold (see Part Four). The moment you mark stress, the GM rolls a D12 and compares it to your total stress across all tracks:

D12 vs. total stress Outcome
Higher than your total No fallout. Stress accumulates.
Equal or lower, and the D12 shows 1–6 Minor fallout. Clear all stress in the marked track.
Equal or lower, and the D12 shows 7–12 Major fallout. Clear all stress from all tracks. Something has permanently changed.

Fallout is the point of the system. It ranges from a passing inconvenience to a permanent change that reshapes who your character is. Sometimes it fires immediately; sometimes it lingers for sessions. The fuller your tracks, the likelier the next mark tips you over — so recklessness is rewarded right up until the bill arrives.

Ships carry their own Hull track — structural damage, crew morale, the ability to keep sailing — separate from personal stress. When the Hull breaks, everyone aboard has a very bad day. The Hull rules live in the wider system documents (Hull & the Ship).

Clearing Stress

Each track clears differently, and that is deliberate — see each track's entry in Part Four for the specifics. In short: Blood clears with rest, food, and a surgeon; Gold clears with plunder and good trades; Reputation clears with public, witnessed deeds (and through bonds and haunts); Tide resists clearing and usually needs ritual or arc-length story work; and Luck cannot be cleared by rest, coin, or carousing at all — it clears only when its fallout finally fires. Luck is the track that does not forgive. It is the mechanical promise at the centre of this game.

Three ways to recover. When a track can be cleared, it happens one of three ways: a Tend roll with the right gear (Part Nine) patches Blood, Gold, or the ship's Hull; a haunt or a bond in a friendly port (Part Eleven) buys back almost anything — including the stubborn Tide and Luck that nothing you can carry will touch; and ordinary rest, plunder, and witnessed deeds clear Blood, Gold, and Reputation over time. If you ever can't find "how do I heal this?", it is in one of those three.

Combat at Character Scale

A fight runs on the same loop as everything else — there is no separate combat minigame. You say what you do; you roll when it's uncertain and costly; the consequence lands. A few things worth stating plainly:

  • No turn order. Nobody waits for initiative. You act when the fiction hands you the spotlight; the enemy hurts you through your failed and partial rolls, and through the GM's framing when you stall or hide (see Timing, below).
  • Taking a foe out. Enemies are adversaries with a Resistance pool (Part Fourteen). You don't track their wounds — you reduce that pool with your successes until it reaches 0, and then they break the way the fiction wants: dead, fled, surrendered, or cowed. Declare what you're trying to make happen; the same statline serves a kill or a capture.
  • Range and position are fiction. Close in and a cutlass (Brave) is the tool; hold the distance and a pistol (Hunt) is. Tags carry the tactics — Reload buys the enemy a moment to close, Ranged lets you hurt what can't yet hurt you, Brutal and Piercing crack a tough target. Getting into the right position is the clever play that steps your difficulty down.
  • Ganging up & helping. Numbers and teamwork show up as difficulty, not extra dice — an ally setting it up, a flanking angle, a distraction can step a Risky strike to Standard. A pack or a squad is one Resistance, not a crowd to be counted.
  • Fighting hurt. Blood fallout — Winded, Limping, Broken (Part Four) — is the price of staying in too long. You can push through it, but the tracks fill and the D12 is always watching. Knowing when to break off is a skill the Shackles teaches the hard way.
  • Surrender, capture, mercy. Not every fight ends in a body. Foes surrender or flee at Resistance 0; so can you. Being taken alive — a press-gang, a Chelish boarding — is a story, not a game over: the Indentured, Seized, or Bounty fallout made flesh.

Full adversary rules and a bestiary are in Part Fourteen; ship-scale battle is Part Ten.

Timing: No Rounds, No Initiative

Play is paced by the fiction, not by a clock. There are no rounds, no turns, and no initiative order — at any scale, including ship-to-ship engagements. Adversaries never "take a turn." They inflict stress through your results (your failures and partial successes) and through the GM's framing when you stall, hide, or do nothing.

The Four Gates

Every recurring ability is limited by exactly one of these — no other gating currencies exist:

  • Once per situation — a single contiguous scene of pressure (a fight, a storm, a negotiation, a delicate job). It ends when the pressure breaks; the GM calls the boundary.
  • Once per session — one real-world sitting of play.
  • Once per journey — the whole passage between two landmarks, however many sessions it takes.
  • Once per campaign — a single use across the entire campaign, never refreshed.

Legal Durations

Timed effects are scoped to the fiction, never to a number of rounds: "your next action," "until you next act," "until the end of the situation," "until the end of the session," "until you next make landfall," or a fictional condition such as "while you remain still" or "until someone acts on it." If an effect needs to repeat — a fire spreading, a storm battering a hull, poison working through a body — it repeats through results: those caught in it mark the listed stress when they fail or partially succeed against it, and whenever the GM judges it simply finds them.

Reading Your Gear

You don't track coins or count shots. A piece of equipment is a stress die (how big a hit it lands or heals) and maybe a tag. No suitable gear means a D4. The ladder runs D4 improvised · D6 civilian · D8 professional · D10 exotic · D12 legendary. The tags you'll meet in a starting kit:

  • Brutal — roll two stress dice, keep the highest.
  • Piercing — ignores armour (Blood protection).
  • Ranged — works at a distance. Reload — needs reloading between shots.
  • Tiring — its die drops a step when you fail with it.
  • Block — +1 Blood protection (the only gear that adds protection).
  • Wyrd — roll the max and you mark Tide; the deep noticed.

The Five Tracks

Stress is the toll the Shackles takes — not a health bar, but the slow accumulation of pain, bad luck, supernatural attention, and lost coin. Every character carries five tracks. The GM marks stress to whichever one the fiction points at, and when a track's fallout fires, something concrete goes wrong. Some of it you can mend. Some of it, you cannot — not until it collects.

BloodThe body

Wounds, exhaustion, fire, drowning, and the shock that lives in the flesh.

Fallout looks like — Winded, Bleeding, Broken (a bone — useless until a surgeon sees it).

Clears by — Rest, food, and a surgeon.

TideThe supernatural got in

Ruins, rites, bargains, the deep paying attention — the rarest and most frightening track.

Fallout looks like — Salt Weeping, The Mark, Compulsion — something must be done, or avoided.

Clears by — The hardest — ritual, or a full arc of story. Rest does nothing.

ReputationYour name

Being bested where it's seen, breaking the Code, a story that travels ahead of you.

Fallout looks like — Whispers, Bounty, Name Destroyed in a stretch of the Shackles.

Clears by — Public, witnessed deeds; standing the room a round; your bonds.

LuckFated misfortune

The universe collecting on audacity, recklessness, and the loose ends you leave behind.

Fallout looks like — Foreboding, then Crisis; The Tab — a forgotten debt come due.

Clears by — Nothing but its own fallout firing. The track that does not forgive.

GoldYour material position

Coin, a prize lost, a bribe paid, gear broken, a debt called in at the worst moment.

Fallout looks like — Light Pockets, Broke, Marked for Collection.

Clears by — Plunder, a good prize, a debt settled.

Skills & Domains

A skill is what you can do; a domain is where you know it and who you know there. When one is relevant, add a die and keep the highest. Both apply? Roll two. Callings — Why You Sail

Your calling is your reason for sailing — the obsession that keeps you from a quiet life ashore. It is not a class or a profession; it is closer to a wound that has set into a direction. Choose the one that names what your character cannot stop wanting; it will deepen as you play, in ways you cannot yet see. Everyone also carries the shared Piracy calling alongside their own.

Calling The pull
Hunger The wanting that the next prize never fills.
Monument Making something — a name, a power — that outlasts you.
Vendetta A single account that has to be settled.
Freedom You were owned once; every cage you meet is yours to break.
Cursed Tides The sea has marked you for its own.
Legacy A name that went to sea before you did.
Fellowship The crew is the only wealth you have ever counted.
Piracy (shared) The life you all chose, and the ledger it keeps.

Zeniths. The grandest beats a calling offers are its zeniths — the height a character's story can climb to. Most pirates sail a long while before they ever reach for one. They are written plainly in each calling below; what it means to reach one, you will understand only when you do.

Choosing Your Calling

If you are torn between two callings, the question tells you which one fits.

  • Hunger or Vendetta? If your target surrendered what they took but walked away free — enough? Yes: Hunger. No, you need to look them in the eye: Vendetta.
  • Hunger or Monument? Hunger consumes and moves on; Monument keeps and builds. Is the question what is over the horizon, or what you will leave behind?
  • Hunger or Cursed Tides? Could you stop? A Hunger character could, in principle. A Cursed Tides character is pulled whether they consent or not.
  • Hunger or Legacy? Begin with nothing and take it, or begin holding something you never earned.
  • Monument or Legacy? Monument constructs weight from nothing; Legacy inherits weight and must decide what to do with it.
  • Monument or Fellowship? Which would you sacrifice first — the friendship or the structure? Monument keeps people because they are useful; Fellowship keeps them because they are irreplaceable.
  • Vendetta or Freedom? If the target died tomorrow: grief at a lost purpose is Vendetta; getting back to work is Freedom. One points at a person, the other at a system.
  • Freedom or Fellowship? Freedom burns a stranger's cage on principle; Fellowship counts the cost to these specific people first. Both are generous — differently.
  • Freedom or Cursed Tides? One refuses every claim; the other is already claimed. (In a single character, this contradiction is fuel, not a problem.)
  • Cursed Tides or Legacy? Can you negotiate with it? A legacy can be refused, honoured, or transformed. The call cannot.
  • Legacy or Vendetta? Vendetta has a heading; Legacy has ballast. One is in pursuit, the other under weight.
  • Legacy or Fellowship? The dead, or the living. Legacy faces what came before; Fellowship faces who is here.

HUNGER

There is always another horizon, and behind it the thing you actually want. You tell yourself this one is the last one. The sea is wide, the take is never enough, and somewhere out there is the prize that will finally fill you up. It won't.

CORE ABILITY

ONE MORE THROW. You have never once accepted the first no. Once per session, after failing a roll in pursuit of something you want, reroll it. If you fail again, the incoming stress steps up one size.

When you create your character, answer these questions:

  • What are you hunting, and what have you already spent on it?
  • Choose another player character. You nearly died together going after something. What was it, and who pulled who out?
  • Choose another player character. What do they own that you cannot stop noticing?
  • Which rival is on the same trail, and how far ahead are you?

MINOR BEATS

  • Take Minor Luck fallout.
  • Take Minor Gold fallout.
  • Steal something while its owner watches.
  • Follow a rumour to a Tier 2 landmark or deeper.
  • Sell or sacrifice a D8 or higher resource to keep the trail warm.
  • Beat a rival to a prize by hours.
  • Shake on a deal you can't honour.
  • Refuse pay you badly need.
  • Spend your last reserve at the worst moment.
  • Acquire a bond who deals in maps, secrets or stolen goods.
  • Tell another PC the whole history of what you hunt.
  • Walk into obvious danger because the prize might be there.
  • Stand somewhere no one on the crew has stood.
  • Learn that something you carry is worth more than you knew.
  • Promise another PC a share to get them aboard a bad idea.
  • Keep a worthless thing you cannot explain keeping.
  • Give away something the moment you finally hold it.
  • Win a haggle you had no business winning.
  • Come back to a place you knew and find it changed beyond recognition.
  • Get caught red-handed.

MAJOR BEATS

  • Take Major Luck fallout.
  • Claim a prize that significant opposition guarded.
  • Sell or sacrifice a D12 resource to learn what waits ahead.
  • Reach a Tier 3 landmark that appears on no reliable chart.
  • Betray an ally for a bigger share.
  • Lose the most valuable thing you own.
  • Learn the prize is cursed, guarded or worse than the stories — and keep going.
  • Hold something the Aspis Consortium will kill to recover.
  • Arrive at last, and find someone got there first.
  • Get another PC to the thing they hunt, at real cost to you.

ZENITH BEATS

  • Claim the thing you have hunted, and pay whatever it asks.
  • Reach it at last — and turn away with empty hands.

MONUMENT

Most pirates take. You are making something — out of ships, stories, favours and fear — and every port that knows your name is a course of bricks laid true. One day the work will stand without you. The trick is living long enough to step back and see it.

CORE ABILITY

BRICK BY BRICK. You strive to live up to the thing you are building. When you gain a minor advance, refresh D6. When you gain a major advance, refresh D8.

When you create your character, answer these questions:

  • What are you building, and what do you call it in your honest moments?
  • Choose another player character. They hold up part of the structure without knowing it. What part?
  • Choose another player character. They knew you when you were nobody. What do you need from them now?
  • Which port already tells a story about you, and how wrong is it?

MINOR BEATS

  • Take Minor Reputation fallout.
  • Be mistaken for something greater than you are.
  • Publicly humiliate a rival, effortlessly.
  • Make a speech meant for the retelling.
  • Hear your story told by someone who doesn't know you're listening.
  • Refuse to retreat — the ground is yours now.
  • Show mercy because the story of it is worth more than the kill.
  • Have a ship change course at the sight of your flag.
  • Donate a D8 or higher resource to a haunt, loudly.
  • Take credit for another pirate's work.
  • Plant influence in a port you've never held: a favour, a debt, a flag.
  • Dictate terms to someone holding the upper hand.
  • Acquire a bond who owes you something they can't repay.
  • Describe the finished work to another PC.
  • Choose a person over the structure, and let a brick fall.
  • Find your name somewhere you never put it.
  • Pick a side in a port dispute because the outcome serves the work.
  • Hear yourself used as the measure of someone else.
  • Pay to make a story go away.
  • Commission a song, a broadsheet or a rumour about yourself.

MAJOR BEATS

  • Take Major Reputation fallout.
  • Enter an enemy power's records: a Chelish mandate, an Aspis dossier.
  • Win formal recognition from the Free Captains or the Pirate Council.
  • Take and hold a berth, a route or an allegiance against opposition.
  • Upgrade a haunt to D10 or higher and put your name on it.
  • Make another PC load-bearing, and tell them what that means.
  • Watch a piece of the structure fall in public.
  • Learn that someone you trusted has been hollowing the walls.
  • Be offered legitimacy — marque, pardon, seat — and decide if it's a brick or a trap.
  • Win the thing you reached for, and feel the foundation crack beneath it.

ZENITH BEATS

  • Step back from what you built, and watch it stand without you.
  • Tear it down yourself, before anyone else can take it.

VENDETTA

They took something from you that cannot be replaced, and they are still breathing. Every prize you take is practice; every port is a place they might be. You will look them in the eye before the end. What happens after that, you have never once let yourself imagine.

CORE ABILITY

NOT WHILE THEY BREATHE. You do not get to fall — not before they do. Once per session, activate this ability to avoid suffering negative effects from Blood or Reputation fallout for the remainder of the situation.

When you create your character, answer these questions:

  • Who are they, what did they take, and how long ago?
  • Choose another player character. They stopped you once, an inch from revenge or ruin. Which was it?
  • Choose another player character. They know the whole story. Why them?
  • What do you carry or wear that your target would recognise on sight?

MINOR BEATS

  • Take Minor Blood fallout.
  • Find someone who knows your target personally.
  • Cost your target something: property, standing, a good lieutenant.
  • Refuse an offered settlement.
  • Learn something that makes your target more human.
  • Put another PC in danger for the vendetta's sake.
  • Meet someone else they hurt.
  • Lie to an ally about where you're leading them.
  • Acquire a bond who feeds you intelligence.
  • Leave a message your target cannot mistake.
  • Chase a lead into waters deeper than the crew agreed to sail.
  • Tell another PC, in full, what was taken.
  • Let hatred make the decision.
  • Hear that your target knows you're coming.
  • Serve someone else's grievance to feed your own.
  • Spend a D8 or higher resource on a lead that dies.
  • Show a mercy your target never would.
  • Rehearse what you'll say when you find them.
  • Learn your target has grown stronger since you started.

MAJOR BEATS

  • Take Major Blood fallout.
  • Destroy a major asset of your target: flagship, stronghold, heir.
  • Fall into your target's hands.
  • Learn something that genuinely complicates the hate.
  • Lasting harm lands on another PC or a bond because of your hunt.
  • Turn down a clean way to end it without facing them.
  • Find out someone hunts you the way you hunt them.
  • Your target strikes first, and the crew bleeds for it.
  • Ally with someone you despise against the common enemy.
  • Win a real victory over them that changes nothing.

ZENITH BEATS

  • Face your target at last, and settle the account.
  • With your target finally in your power, let them go.

FREEDOM

Someone owned you once — by law, by debt, by force — and you remember exactly what it weighed. Now you are salt and open water, and every cage you meet is yours to break. You call it principle. Some nights it feels more like the chain, still pulling.

CORE ABILITY

NEVER AGAIN. You carried the weight once. Never again. Once per session, allocate stress you would mark to the nearest friendly target — PC or NPC — instead of marking it yourself.

When you create your character, answer these questions:

  • Who held the leash, and what do they still hold — papers, family, a price on you?
  • How did you get free, and who didn't make it out with you?
  • Choose another player character. One of you freed the other. Which way around, and what's owed?
  • Choose another player character. They've watched you risk the whole crew for a stranger in chains. What did they say?

MINOR BEATS

  • Take Minor Blood fallout.
  • Free a prisoner, thrall or pressed sailor.
  • Refuse a direct order from someone you respect.
  • Burn a contract, writ or bill of sale.
  • Be caught, bound or held — briefly.
  • Meet someone who loves their cage.

Castaway

The Shackles is a wild place, and it keeps the people it wrecks. Every island chain has its stories: the sailor who walked out of the interior two years after their ship went down, eating things no one could name, speaking a little less than they used to. These survivors are known as Castaways. The shipwreck was just how they arrived — what changed them was everything after. They ate what grew in the deep interior. They drank from springs beneath Ghol-Gan stonework. They slept in ruins because the stone held heat and the things in the trees did not come inside. And the wild Shackles, which does not care about names or flags or the Pirate Code, decided to keep them functional — remaking them, gradually and without explanation, into something more suitable. Castaways are prized aboard any ship that strays from the charted lanes. No chart, no pilot and no priest can tell you as much about an island as someone who has been eaten by one and come out the other side. They do not always come ashore willingly. But put them on a beach with a knife and a hungry interior ahead of them, and watch them become most completely themselves.

Chronicler

The Shackles has been drowning civilisations since before it had a name. Azlant sank whole. The cyclops empire of Ghol-Gan left its stonework on every island, geometries that do not quite resolve into sense. Lirgen, nation of astrologers, watched the sky for a thousand years and never saw the Eye coming. The sea swallows them all, and the Eye grinds what remains into something half-preserved and half-destroyed — and you read what they left behind. You learned early what the colonial universities refuse to admit: that the magic you practise and the history you study are the same subject. A dead tradition is not gone. Its workings are still down there in the wrecks and the ruin-chambers, written in scripts no living person was supposed to read, waiting for someone with the patience to recover them and the nerve to speak them aloud. The other scholars call what you do grave-robbery. The grave-robbers call it scholarship. They are both correct, and neither of them can do what you do: stand in a drowned temple, read the wall, and make the past pick its tools back up.

Powder Mage

Sawbone

The saw that opens a chest cavity and the saw that cuts a rotten plank are the same saw. The pins that hold a shattered femur together and the pins that splice a split timber are the same gauge. You worked out early that a ship's carpenter and a ship's surgeon are doing the same job — assess the damage, remove what cannot be saved, reinforce what can, return the patient to function — and the Shackles has spent your whole career confirming it. You have taken off arms below decks during a broadside and crawled into the bilge to patch shot-holes in the same afternoon, and the problems felt identical. The others aboard traffic in pacts and omens and reputations; you traffic in copper, tar, catgut and load. When the witch's familiar dies, when the god goes quiet, when the luck runs out — they bring it to you, because you are the only one aboard whose power does not have an opinion. And underneath the daily work there is the project: the thing you are building that the Shackles says is impossible. The hand that closes when the forearm flexes. The pump no flooding can outrun. Sharper

Every Free Captain thinks they understand the game — raids, plunder, loyalty, fear. You understand the game that happens underneath that game. Who is sleeping with whom. Which Pirate Council member owes a debt to which merchant house. Which harbourmaster is on three separate payrolls, and which informant changed sides on Thursday. You have built an empire of knowledge that nobody can see, and you protect it the way a spider protects a web: by being invisible, and by ensuring that anything that touches it gets stuck. The cards, the dice, the cons — those are the visible trade, and you are very good at them, but they were only ever apprenticeship. The real game taught you its one true rule early: everything in the Shackles is a wager, every person is a hand to be read, and the house — given enough patience, enough ledgers, enough friends in enough back rooms — the house is you. You only bet on certainties. The trick is manufacturing them.

CORE ABILITIES

THE SURE THING

Fate plays cards like everyone else; you palm its best card and keep it up your sleeve. At any time, you may consume a resource and roll its dice; set the result aside. When you perform an action or inflict stress, but before you roll the dice to resolve it, you may replace any unrolled dice in your pool with the die you set aside. Once used in this manner, it is spent. If you have an unspent die set aside at the end of the session, mark Luck stress equal to the value showing on it.

DOUBLE OR NOTHING

Fate plays cards like everyone else, and like everyone else, it can be hustled. At any time, roll a D6. If the result is equal to or under your current Luck stress, clear that much stress from Luck and roll with mastery on your next action. If it's over, add that much stress to Luck.

  • NEVER WAS. On a night's notice, the room vanishes: furniture, records, staff, all of it. Patrons misremember the address. Investigators find a storeroom that has clearly been empty for years.

Sin Bosun

Every ship has a sin bosun, whether the muster book records one or not. The crew might call them the padre, the holy, the god-botherer, the one who talks to the sky — what they mean is: the person you find when someone is dying, and also the person you find when you are lost. In the Shackles these have always been the same job, because the sea does not distinguish between a body it is killing and a ship it is sinking. You serve your god by moving through the world they made — Besmara who owns every plank that touches her water, Gozreh who is the wind in the sail and the storm against the hull, or something older and less named that speaks in current and depth. Your theology is practical: the god's domain is the sea, and to navigate it correctly — to read its weather, to know its routes, to bring ships and souls through it alive — is an act of worship. The chart is the scripture. The stars are the congregation. The passage completed is the prayer answered. You are not a soft priest. The sea is not kind, your god is not kind, and the mercy you offer is the specific, practical mercy of someone who has studied how to get people through things. You know the route. You know the rite. You know, better than most, that the difference between them is mostly a matter of what is doing the killing.

Tidecaller

The Eye of Abendego did not form by accident. Something lives at the heart of that immortal storm — ancient, patient, and interested in very specific things. You found it, or it found you. The terms were unclear. They remain unclear. What is clear is what you got: the wild magic of the ocean and the bargained, familiar-haunted power of the witch tradition, braided into one trade — and what it is costing you, a little at a time. Your blood runs colder than it used to. Salt crusts where you sleep. There are mornings when your reflection takes a moment too long to do what you did. Crews fear you and captains court you, because a witch aboard is luck and weather and hexes turned aside, and everyone has heard what happens to ships that put one over the side. You are treated as something between an officer and an omen. They are right to do so. You are not entirely sure, anymore, which side of the bargain you are standing on — whether you are a person who commands the sea's power, or a place where the sea is slowly coming ashore.

Ancestries — Who You Are

Your ancestry is who you are before the dice hit the table — where you come from, how the world has treated you, what ghosts you carry onto a pirate deck. It grants no rules; it is who you are, not what you can do.

Two kinds of ancestry

The peoples of the Shackles fall into two groups, and they answer the "who are you" question differently.

Cultures are for the "baseline" folk of the Inner Sea — humans, elves, half-elves, and the like. For these peoples, species barely matters; what matters is the culture that raised you. There is no "human" or "elf" ancestry in this game — instead you choose a culture pool, and your exact species within it is yours to picture. A Chelaxian may be human or elf; a Shackleborn is whatever blood the islands have mixed. Pick the upbringing, not the bloodline.

Kindreds are peoples set apart by their very nature — their bodies, instincts, or origins mark them out, and they carry their own culture with them wherever the tide takes them. A kindred is your people.

Shackleborn

A culture, not a species. There is no "human" or "elf" ancestry in this game — those peoples are defined by the culture that raised them, and the Shackleborn are whatever blood the islands have mixed: human, elf, half-elf, and every other baseline folk born to the tide.

You were born somewhere between two tides, on an island that flies no nation's flag, to parents who were themselves the leavings of a dozen coasts — a little Chelish, a little Mwangi, a little Tian, a little of whatever sailor passed through the season you were made. The Shackleborn are not an ancestry of blood but of place: the creole people of the islands, raised on salt fish and the Pirate Code, who learned to swim before they learned to walk and to lie before they learned to read. You have never had a homeland to lose, because the sea between the islands is the only home there has ever been. Outsiders call the Shackles lawless; you know it has more laws than anywhere, they are simply not written down, and breaking them costs more. You do not dream of escaping to a better life elsewhere — there is no elsewhere that would have you, and you would not go.

Background Questions

  • You came up in the alleys and wharves of Port Peril, running messages and lifting purses before you could shave. Whose crew or gang raised you in place of the parents you barely remember — and what do you still owe them?
  • You were one of dozens of children aboard a single great pirate vessel, raised collectively by a crew who were closer than any blood. Something happened to that ship and most of that crew. What was it, and what do you carry from it?
  • You grew up in a tiny anchorage on a forgotten island, far from Quent or any place that matters, and left the day a ship would take you. What were you running toward, and what did you swear you'd never become?

Names

Shackleborn names are magpie things, stitched from every culture that ever made port: a Chelish given name, a Mwangi surname, a nickname earned in a tavern that outlives both. Most go by the nickname. Examples: Cinder, Marisol Okonkwo, Tam Greel, Quintana, Bilge-Rat Ojo, Sefa "Two-Knives" Castellan.

Many Shackleborn are renamed at least once by a crew — a deed-name or an insult that stuck. The birth name becomes a thing you only tell people you trust.

Bonuwat

A culture, not a species. Most Bonuwat are human, but the coast has long taken in elves, half-elves, and others who learned its waters; what makes you Bonuwat is the shore that raised you, not the shape of your blood.

Your people are the coast itself made into a nation — the seafaring folk of the western Mwangi shore, who were reading the currents and free-diving the reefs of these waters long before the first Chelish keel cut through them. To be Bonuwat is to belong to the sea in a way the colonisers will never understand and never stop trying to buy. You know the names of currents the charts do not record, the reefs that move, the islands that are only there at certain tides. The Aspis Consortium and the Chelish navy carved up your coast for timber, ivory, and worse, and called it trade; your grandparents called it what it was. Some of your people went pirate to take it all back one prize at a time; some went pirate because there was nothing left of the old life to stay for. Either way, the deep remembers you, and you have never once felt alone on the water.

Background Questions

  • A slaving operation working out of Bloodcove took people from your village — perhaps including you, before you got free. You sail in part to make that trade bleed. What did you lose to it, and how close have you come to the ones responsible?
  • You were a pilot and free-diver whose knowledge of the reefs was worth a fortune, and a Free Captain offered you a berth rather than a chain for it. Which captain, and do you trust the bargain you struck?
  • You left the coast willingly, drawn by a story the elders tell about something out beyond the charted water — a drowned place, a debt the sea is owed, a song under the waves. What did you go looking for, and has the sea begun to answer?

Names

Bonuwat names carry weight — a birth name from the family, and often a second name earned from the sea itself: a thing you survived, a creature you faced, a reef you alone can run. Examples: Adaeze, Kofi Reef-Walker, Ngozi, Seun, Yetunde Deep-Breath, Abara Two-Tides.

Many Bonuwat sailors take a sea-name in addition to their birth name; it is the sea-name that is used among a crew, and the birth name that is used among kin.

Chelaxian

A culture, not a species. Chelish society is mostly human, with old elven and half-elven houses woven through its bloodlines; you are Chelaxian by upbringing and bearing, whatever your ancestry beneath it.

You were raised in the long shadow of the most disciplined and devil-haunted empire of the Inner Sea — pale, schooled, and taught from infancy that order is worth any price, including your soul and everyone else's. Cheliax made you to be useful: a naval officer, a factor's clerk, a duelist of good family, a cog in a machine that runs on contracts signed in something darker than ink. And then something broke the spell — a press-ganging, a disgrace, a conscience that arrived too late or right on time — and you ran for the one place in the world the empire cannot reach. You brought your education with you, which is more useful at sea than you would like to admit; you can forge a manifest, read a man-o-war's signals, and pass for a magistrate's adjutant in a way no Free Captain ever could. But the accent marks you, and so does the flinch when someone mentions a name you used to answer to.

Background Questions

  • You served aboard a Chelish man-o-war out of Corentyn before your ship was taken — and the Free Captains offered you a berth instead of the deep. What did you do to earn that, when your fellow officers earned a knife?
  • You fled a contract — an indenture, a betrothal, a debt sworn before a devil — that is still, technically, legally binding. Who in Cheliax still holds your paper, and what will it cost when they send someone to collect?
  • You came to the Shackles believing in the empire and meaning to bring it order, and somewhere along the way you changed sides for real. What did you see that turned you, and who from your old life would put a price on your head if they knew?

Names

Chelish names are formal, layered, and faintly archaic — a given name, a house name, often a saint's or ancestor's name carried like a debt. Out here you have probably shortened it, or thrown away the house name entirely. Examples: Aurelio Thrune-by-marriage, Severa, Maddox Vell, Cordelia Anvanus, Just "Vespers," Tace Drovost.

A Chelish family name still opens doors and closes throats across the Inner Sea. Many defectors drop theirs and keep only a given name — but the ones hunting them know the old one.

Small Folk

A kindred, not one people: halflings, gnomes, dwarves, and every other small civilised folk the Inner Sea reckons "ordinary" — as opposed to the goblins and kobolds it calls vermin.

You are one of the small peoples — the ones the tall world finds useful, underestimates, and forgets to fear. Maybe you are a halfling, born into a Chelish household that owned you or one generation removed from one, who got over a wall and never looked back. Maybe you are a gnome, fey-touched and restless, forever one new wonder ahead of the grey creeping numbness your people call the Bleaching, and the deck of a pirate ship is the most interesting thing you have ever found. Maybe you are a dwarf, exiled from the stone-holds by oath or feud or sheer wanderlust, carrying your craft and your grudges out onto a sea your ancestors never trusted. What you share is not blood but position: you are small in a world built for the big, and you have learned that being overlooked is a kind of armour, and that the small hands get into the places the large ones cannot.

Background Questions

  • You came up under a roof that did not consider you a full person — a Chelish estate near Corentyn, an Aspis counting-house in Bloodcove, a dwarf-hold that cast you out. Who decided your size was the same as your worth, and how close have they come to finding you since?
  • You are chasing something the tall world doesn't value enough to guard properly — a wonder, a craft, a freedom, a debt of honour older than anyone alive. What is it, and what has the chase already cost you?
  • You were born free in the Shackles, small among giants, and learned early that the deck doesn't care how tall you are if your hands are quick and your nerve holds. Who taught you that, and what did you have to prove to them?

Names

Small Folk names span their peoples: halfling names are warm and plain, often a trade or a place (Pim Saltcellar, Lula, Mosey); gnome names are long, musical, and collected like keepsakes, usually shortened by everyone else (Fonkin, Zither, Bee); dwarf names are hard and clan-bound, an honorific or a hold attached (Hekka Stonebound, Durgan, Vess Oath-Kept). Examples across the kindred: Pim, Bee Quick-Fingers, Durgan No-Hold, Hessa Freeborn, Tock, Corrin Tallows.

Many Small Folk carry two names — the one their people gave them and the one the tall world can be bothered to pronounce. Halflings born to bondage often choose a free name on getting out; gnomes change theirs whenever the old one stops delighting them.

Goblinkin

A kindred, not one people: goblins, kobolds, and the other small folk the Inner Sea does not call "ordinary" but "vermin" — and hunts accordingly.

You are small like the halflings and gnomes, but where the world merely overlooks them, it actively wants you dead. Maybe you are a goblin — sharp-toothed, fire-mad, gleefully alive — raised in a warren that the nearest town burned out at least once in your memory, who learned that the only safe place is moving and the only currency is being too useful or too funny to kill. Maybe you are a kobold — scaled, clever, and proud — bred to traps and tunnels and the worship of things much larger than yourself, until you slipped your warren's hierarchy and struck out to be nobody's small and expendable cog. The respectable peoples treat you as a problem with legs; the Free Captains, gloriously, do not care, so long as you can fit down the hawsehole to clear a fouled cable, rig a trap no boarder expects, or do the loud dangerous thing nobody taller will volunteer for. You have spent your whole life being underestimated and hunted. On a pirate deck, for the first time, the first half is an advantage and the second half is just Tuesday.

Background Questions

  • A town, a baron, or a "pest-control" bounty out of a colonial port like Bloodcove put a price on your whole warren — maybe on you specifically. What happened to the rest of them, and what did you do that they still remember?
  • You are dangerously, delightedly good at the thing that scares everyone else — fire, traps, heights, the powder magazine, the narrow flooded crawlspace. What's your specialty, and what has it already cost you or someone near you?
  • You attached yourself to this crew the way your kind always survives: by being indispensable to someone bigger. Who did you decide to be useful to, do they know it's mutual, and what happens the day they stop needing you?

Names

Goblin names are short, percussive, and often gross or boastful, earned and re-earned by deeds (Snag, Chuff, Reekfinger, Last); kobold names hiss and click, frequently invoking a draconic patron or a clever trap they're proud of (Skritt, Vex, Az-the-Snare, Little Tooth). Examples across the kindred: Snag, Skritt, Reek, Vex Trapwright, Chuff, Az.

Goblins rename themselves constantly — after a meal, a fire, a near-death — and sing the new name until it sticks. Kobolds guard their true-name and offer the surface a use-name, often the name of the trap or trick they're best at.

Big Folk

A kindred, not one people: half-orcs, minotaurs, hobgoblins, and the other large, strong, martial folk the Inner Sea greets with a drawn weapon — and a job offer.

You are one of the big peoples, built on a scale the polite world finds threatening and the working world finds invaluable. Maybe you are a half-orc, born from a violence or a love the cities cannot bear to look at, too orc for the humans and too human for the orcs, welcome nowhere a roof keeps off the rain. Maybe you are a minotaur, raised among the old labyrinth-ruins, carrying a strength and a shame and a sense of direction that never fails even when everything else does. Maybe you are a hobgoblin, bred for a war-society of iron discipline and brutal order, who looked at the rank ahead of you and the chain around your neck and decided to desert the whole arrangement. What you share is that the Inner Sea taught you your body was a problem to be managed — and then you found a deck, where the only questions are can you haul, can you fight, will you hold the line, and for the first time the answer being yes made you an asset instead of an omen.

Background Questions

  • You were muscle for someone — a Chelish workhouse, a hobgoblin legion, a gang on the docks of a colonial port — until you decided your strength would only ever work for you again. Who did you break with to get free, and are they still looking?
  • You carry a discipline or a code from where you came up — a soldier's order, a labyrinth-keeper's patience, a half-orc's hard-won restraint — that the chaos of piracy tests every single day. What is it, and what happens on the day it finally breaks?
  • You are trying to prove something — that you are more than the fist people reach for, more than the monster the stories promised. What's the thing you're chasing, and what will it take before you believe you've done it?

Names

Examples across the kindred: Grust, Oggun Pier-Breaker, Mara, Korthax of the Sunken Maze, Vell, Iron-Twelve "Twelve."

A Big Folk raised among humans often carries a deliberately unremarkable name; one raised among a crew often wears the name the crew gave them, like a flag. A deserter hobgoblin's first free act is usually choosing a name that is not a number.

The Made

A kindred, not one people: androids, automatons, homunculi, and the other thinking things that were built rather than born — and woke up anyway.

You were not born. You were made — assembled, cast, grown in a vat, wound up, or breathed into being by someone who wanted a tool and got a person instead. Maybe you are an android, a synthetic body of ageless flesh and buried machinery, carrying a soul that has worn other bodies before this one and will move on after, hunting in this single lifetime for what it means to be a self and not a thing. Maybe you are an automaton, a frame of brass and old enchantment, a soldier or a servant whose makers are long dust, still walking because no one ever told you that you could stop. Maybe you are a homunculus, a small made-thing built to serve a master who is dead, or worse, not dead, and still believes they own you. The Inner Sea cannot decide whether you are property, miracle, or abomination, and reaches for a different one of the three depending on who can profit. The Shackles, which is built entirely of people who refused to be owned, is the one place that looks at a made thing claiming itself a free person and simply says: prove it on the next watch.

Background Questions

  • You had a maker or an owner — a Chelish artificer, a Numerian salvage-crew, a wizard whose tower you walked out of. Are they dead, hunting you, or worst of all still legally entitled to you under Inner Sea law, and how close have they come to collecting?
  • You are trying to become — or to prove you already are — a real person, with all the mess that entails. What specifically are you chasing (a feeling, a choice, a death you can call your own), and what have you done that frightened you by how alive it was?
  • The crew took a while to decide you weren't a clever machine playing at a soul. Who vouched for you first, what did you do to earn it, and what do you owe them now?

Names

The Made are named in three ways: a maker's designation, dry and functional (Unit Seven, Cataloguer, Vessel-9); a name a Made chooses on waking, often abstract or aspirational (Vigil, Ash, Nine, Tuesday, Hope); or a name a crew gives them, affectionate and plain (Tin, Brass, Doc). Examples: Vigil, Unit Seven (now just "Seven"), Ash, Brass, Cataloguer, Tuesday.

Many of the Made keep their maker's designation the way a freed person keeps an old scar — not from love, but to remember. Choosing a new name is, for most, the first act that was truly their own.

Beast-folk

A kindred, not one people: tengu, catfolk, ratfolk, merfolk, naga, and every other folk who wear an animal's shape and an animal's instincts under a thinking mind.

You are one of the beast-folk — a person the tall, smooth, hairless world calls an it before it calls you anything else, and you have a creature's patience for that particular insult. Maybe you are tengu, crow-folk who live in the cracks of other people's cities and collect words the way magpies collect bright things. Maybe you are catfolk of the Amurrun, curious and proud and built for the rigging, landing on your feet out of habits older than language. Maybe you are ratfolk of the Ysoki, a tinker and a hoarder and a survivor who can fit through a gap the size of your own skull and has done it more than once. Maybe you are merfolk, the sea's own, who can only walk ashore by a bargain and pays for every hour of it. Maybe you are naga, serpentine and uncanny and far older than you look, carrying a coil of ancient memory the human world finds frightening. What you share is the animal under the skin — the instinct that fires before thought, the senses that read a room or a reef before anyone speaks — and the knowledge that the sea, at least, treats every kind of creature with the same honest indifference.

Background Questions

  • You worked a great port — Port Peril, Quent, somewhere busier and crueller — in the spaces the tall folk overlook: the rigging, the bilges, the rumour-rich dark of the wharves. What did your senses catch there that you weren't meant to, and who's still angry about it?
  • Your body answers to the sea in a way the others' don't — gills, claws, a tail, a coil, an instinct for current and weather — and it is both your gift and your leash. What can you do that they can't, and what does it cost you that they'll never understand?
  • You come from a beast-folk community with its own ways and its own long memory — a tengu wharf-clan, an Amurrun pride, an Ysoki warren, a drowned merfolk court, a naga's ancient solitude. What does it expect of you out here, and what do you owe it?

Names

Beast-folk names follow their peoples and their instincts: tengu collect good words and onomatopoeia (Kaw, Rattle, Two-Penny); catfolk take lithe, proud names, often a hunt-name earned (Sefu, Quick-Paw, Nadira); ratfolk run to practical, scurrying names and nicknames (Tisk, Gnaw, Pellet); merfolk and naga carry long, liquid, half-unpronounceable true-names and give the surface a short one (Vael, Ssira, the one they call "Tide"). Examples across the kindred: Kaw, Sefu Nine-Lives, Tisk, Vael of the Drowned Court, Ssirahl "Tide," Hark.

Most beast-folk keep a true-name in their own tongue and hand the surface world a deck-name — a sound, a trait, a word they liked. The true-name is for kin and for the sea; the deck-name is for everyone else, and may change with the voyage.

Planetouched

A kindred, not one people: tieflings, aasimars, ifrits, undines, oreads, sylphs, and everyone else born with a plane in their blood — marked, at birth, by a heritage they did not choose.

Somewhere up your bloodline a door was left open — to the Hells, to the heavens, to the elemental deeps where fire and water and stone are alive — and it left its sign on you. Maybe you are a tiefling, fiend-blooded, born with horns or hooves or eyes like coals into a Cheliax that breeds the infernal into its noble lines and then despises what it makes. Maybe you are an aasimar, touched by something brighter, with a glow under the skin and a destiny everyone keeps trying to write for you. Maybe you are elemental-blooded — an ifrit who runs hot and angry, an undine whose skin is always faintly wet, an oread heavy and slow as the seabed, a sylph the wind seems to follow — kin to forces older than any god's quarrel. What you all share is that strangers read your birth off your face before you open your mouth, and decide they already know what you are. They are wrong, and proving it is the work of a life. The Shackles, which is full of people refusing to be the thing they were born into, is the one sea where your blood is just one more rumour about you, no truer than the rest.

Background Questions

  • You were born into a Chelish house that dabbles in the infernal, and your blood paid for someone else's bargain — a mark, a debt, an expectation. Whose deal are you carrying, and what did you do when you decided it was not yours to honour?
  • Your heritage showed early, in a Shackles port that took it for an omen — good or ill — and treated you accordingly ever since. Where, what did they decide you were, and how have you used or fought that ever since?
  • The plane in your blood is calling — a pull toward the Eye, the deep, a far fire, a voice that claims you as kin — and you are not sure whether to answer it or drown it. What calls you, and what have you already done to keep from going?

Names

Planetouched names run three ways: a name given for the mark (hellish epithets, luminous celestial names, element-words); a "virtue name" a family hangs on a tiefling child like an apology (Mercy, Temperance, Owed); or a plain name chosen in defiance of all of it. Examples across the kindred: Ember, Mercy Vane, Tide-in-the-Blood, Cassiel, Stone, Just "Smoke."

Many planetouched carry the name their blood earned them and a second they chose — and answer to whichever tells them more about the person using it.

Make Your Pirate

Make your pirate.

A character in the Shackles is built from three pieces and a handful of details. The pieces answer three different questions, and none of them answers the other two:

  • Class — what can you do? The toolkit that has kept you alive. (Part Six.)
  • Calling — why do you sail? The obsession that keeps you from a quiet life ashore. (Part Five.)
  • Ancestry — who are you, and where are you from? Purely who you are in the fiction; it grants no mechanics. (Step 4, below.)

Work through the steps in order. By the end you will have a character, a place at a crew, and a reason the sea will one day come to collect. Pencil, not pen — some of this is decided together with the table.

Step 1 — Concept

One sentence. A press-ganged Chelish navigator who found God in a storm and never went home. A dockside cardsharp building an empire of favours. A thing the jungle spat back out. You do not need a backstory yet — the class, calling, and ancestry will hand you most of one. You need a hook you are excited to play.

Step 2 — Choose a Class

Your class is your competence. Pick one of the ten (Part Six) and take everything in its Core Traits:

  1. Its core skill and core domain — your starting skill and domain. (Everyone can attempt most things; a skill or domain adds a die and means you are genuinely good at it. See Part Three.)
  2. Its resource — a single evocative item with a value and domain.
  3. Its equipment — take the listed gear, and pick one from the choice the class offers.
  4. Both of its core abilities — the powers that define the class. You have these from the start.

You do not take its other abilities yet — those come in play. You begin lean and grow into the legend.

Step 3 — Choose a Calling

Your calling is your engine. Pick one of the seven personal callings (Part Five); if you are torn, the choosing guide settles it. Then:

  1. Take its core ability.
  2. Answer its four questions. Two of them name other player characters — do this at the table, out loud, so your stories are already tangled before play begins.
  3. Roll (or choose) on its d10 carry table — the object you bring to mark this life. It is not equipment. It is evidence.

Everyone also holds the shared Piracy calling alongside their personal one; you will build its side of things with the whole crew in Step 7.

Step 4 — Choose an Ancestry

Your ancestry is who you are before the dice hit the table. It grants no mechanics — no bonuses, no abilities, no protection. The storm does not care whether you are a half-orc or a Chelish noble; what matters is the grudges, loyalties, and reasons you are standing on a deck at the edge of the world.

An ancestry is built from a short lore block, three background questions, a set of names, and a d20 trinkets table. To make a character: read your ancestry's lore, answer one of its three background questions, choose a name, and roll twice on its trinkets table (keep both).

The Shackles ancestries are in Part Seven, in two groups: cultures (Shackleborn, Bonuwat, Chelaxian — for humans, elves, and the like, who are defined by their upbringing rather than their species) and kindreds (Small Folk, Goblinkin, Big Folk, The Made, Beast-folk, Planetouched — peoples set apart by their very nature). Pick one; more are easily built with the Ancestry Design Guide. If none fits, describe where you are from and answer three questions freeform — it costs you nothing mechanically either way.

Step 5 — Your Resistances

You have five resistance tracks (Part Four): Blood, Tide, Reputation, Luck, Gold. They all start empty — a fresh character carries no stress.

Protection reduces stress before it is marked. You start with 0 protection in every track unless a class ability or your equipment's Block tag says otherwise; protection is earned through advances, not bought as armour. Note any protection your starting abilities grant.

Step 6 — Bonds

You may begin with one or two bonds — real relationships in the Shackles who carry your story when you are not in the room (a quartermaster who owes you, a tavern-keeper who keeps your seat, a rival who would be insulted if anyone else killed you). A bond is a strong relationship, not necessarily a kind one. You can hold at most three; leave room to make more in play. (Full bond rules in Part Eleven.)

Step 7 — The Crew, the Ship & the Home Port

Piracy is not a solo act. As a table, build the thing you all share:

  1. Answer the four crew questions from the Piracy calling together — the ship and what she cost, your first prize and what went wrong, your home port, and the enemy who already knows your flag.
  2. Define the ship — her size and quality (Part Ten: Ships of the Shackles). A sound working vessel starts at Hull protection 1; that is plenty to begin.
  3. Name a home port — a friendly landmark you can return to, heal at, and be hunted in (Part Eleven).

Step 8 — Finishing Touches

Name, look, voice, the flag you sail under. Write your two starting active beats (one or two — chosen each session thereafter from your calling or the Piracy pool; see Part Five). Then fill in your character sheet and you are ready to sail.

Advancement — How You Grow

You grow by hitting beats, not by killing things or banking experience.

  • At the end of each session you choose two active beats from your personal calling or the shared Piracy calling (the pools combine). You can hold only two at a time, and cannot hit more than one in a single situation.
  • When you hit a beat, take an advance from your class at the matching level:
  • Minor beat → minor advance. Most of your growth: a new skill, a new domain, +2 protection, or a named minor ability. Three of each class's minor advances are generic and repeatable (pick a skill, pick a domain, or +1 protection).
  • Major beat → major advance. A larger power. Each major ability has three sub-options you can take as later advances to deepen it.
  • Zenith beat → zenith advance. A legendary, transformative power — the summit your character can reach. Rare, and never taken lightly; reaching for one tells the table your pirate's legend is cresting.
  • Gaining a skill or domain a second time grants a knack (Part Three) — roll that narrow thing with mastery.

The shape of advancement is the shape of the story. Minor advances are the texture of the good years; majors are the turning points; the zeniths are the summit — the moment a pirate becomes a tale the Shackles tells long after. What waits at that height, you will learn only by climbing to it.

Quick Reference — The Build

  1. Concept — one sentence.
  2. Class — core skill, core domain, resource, equipment (pick one), both core abilities.
  3. Calling — core ability, answer four questions, roll a carry item.
  4. Ancestry — lore, answer one question, name, roll two trinkets. (No mechanics.)
  5. Resistances — five tracks empty; protection 0 unless an ability grants it.
  6. Bonds — one or two to start (max three).
  7. Crew & ship — answer the Piracy crew questions; set ship size/quality and a home port.
  8. Finish — name, look, two active beats, fill the sheet.

Quick Reference

The Roll

Roll a pool of D10s, keep the single highest. One die always, plus:

  • +1 die for a relevant skill
  • +1 die for a relevant domain
  • +1 die (mastery) if a knack applies — max 4 dice
Highest die Result
10 Critical success — what you wanted, plus extra. Step up your outgoing stress dice.
8–9 Success — what you wanted. No stress.
6–7 Success at a cost — you get it, but take stress or a complication.
2–5 Failure — take stress; it goes wrong.
1 Critical failure — take double stress.

Difficulty: Standard (keep highest) · Risky (drop the highest die first) · Dangerous (drop the two highest). Good/excellent gear, a plan, position, or help can step it down. Roll only when the outcome is uncertain and failure costs something.

Stress & Fallout

On a 2–7, mark stress to a track (the GM picks the track from the fiction, not the skill). Dice by severity: D4 minor · D6 serious · D8 dangerous · D10 catastrophic. Protection reduces the rolled value first.

Then roll a D12 vs. your total stress across all tracks:

D12 vs. total Outcome
Higher No fallout — it accumulates.
≤ total, D12 = 1–6 Minor fallout — clear the marked track.
≤ total, D12 = 7–12 Major fallout — clear all tracks; something changes for good.

The Five Tracks

Track Is Clears by
Blood wounds, exhaustion, drowning, fire rest, food, a surgeon
Tide the supernatural getting in ritual, arc-length story (hardest)
Reputation your name and standing public deeds, bonds, haunts
Luck fated misfortune only when its fallout fires
Gold your material position plunder, good trades

Equipment can never remove Tide, Luck, or Reputation.

The Four Gates

Recurring abilities use exactly one: once per situation · per session · per journey · per campaign. No rounds, no initiative — durations are scoped to the fiction.

Equipment

Roll the dice on the gear; no suitable gear = D4. Ladder: D4 improvised · D6 civilian · D8 professional · D10 exotic · D12 legendary. Quality (separate axis): Standard / Good / Excellent / Renowned (Renowned grants a knack). Weapons tagged Brave / Hunt / Plunder; journey gear Navigate; healing Tend Blood/Gold/Hull.

Common tags: Brutal (roll 2 stress dice, keep highest) · Piercing (ignores Blood protection) · Ranged · Reload · Tiring (dice drops on a fail) · Block (+1 Blood protection) · Spread (catches nearby targets) · Wyrd (max roll → mark Tide).

Skills & Domains

Skills (11): Brave · Carouse · Command · Deceive · Endure · Hunt · Navigate · Plunder · Reckon · Tend · Vanish.

Domains (9): Deepwater · Shallows · Port · Outlaw · Colonial · Arcane · Faith · Ruins · Wilderness.

A skill is what you can do; a domain is where you know it. Gain either a second time → a knack (roll that narrow thing with mastery).
