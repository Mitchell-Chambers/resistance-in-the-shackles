# Part Thirteen — Running the Game

*Front matter for the GM. Everything here gathers rulings made elsewhere in this reference into one place and tells you how to hold them at the table. If a rule and this chapter ever disagree, the rule wins; this is the how, not the what.*

You are not the players' opponent and you are not a referee enforcing a rulebook. You are the Shackles itself — the wind, the water, the crowd on the dock, the patient enemy on the horizon, and the long memory of a sea that always, eventually, collects. Your job is to make the liberty feel real and the reckoning feel earned. The good times are the bait; the doom is the hook; the players supply the recklessness that drives them onto it. This chapter is about running that bargain honestly.

---

## 1. The GM's job, in order

In any given moment, you are doing one of these:

1. **Frame the scene.** Say where they are, who's there, what's wrong or about to be, and what the pressure is. Hand the spotlight to a player and ask what they do.
2. **Voice the world.** Play the NPCs and the weather as if they want things. The Shackles is crowded with appetites; let them collide.
3. **Set the stakes, then call for a roll** — but only when both a real consequence and a real uncertainty are present (§3).
4. **Read the result and narrate the consequence.** On anything but a clean success, *something happens*: stress lands, a complication opens, the world moves. Make it concrete.
5. **Keep the doom warm.** Track the Luck that's accruing, the beats the players have chosen, the Downfalls they're sailing toward — and bend the fiction so those things can land when the time comes.

## 2. Principles

- **Fiction first, then dice.** Establish what's actually happening before anyone rolls. The roll resolves a situation the fiction has already made tense; it never creates the tension by itself.
- **Make the world react.** Consequences should be things the world *does*, not numbers it subtracts. A failed Vanish isn't "−3 stealth," it's *the deck-watch turns at the wrong moment*.
- **Let them be magnificent first.** This game only hurts if the players have something to lose. Early on, let the plans work, the prizes come in, the legend grow. Spend that goodwill later.
- **The doom is patient.** You never need to rush the reckoning. Luck accrues on its own; the callings point at their own endings. Your job is to *not look away* when the bill is ready, not to send it early.
- **Play to find out.** You don't know how a character's story ends — that's the player's to discover through their beats and their choices. Don't pre-write the tragedy; set the conditions and let it happen.
- **Stress is a clock, not a wound.** Marking stress is good pacing — it means the situation is costing something. Don't be stingy with it, and don't apologise for it; the fallout it builds toward is the point.
- **Say yes to audacity, charge for it.** When a player reaches for the spectacular, let them try. The price is a harder difficulty, a track that fills, a Luck mark that will come due — not a "no."
- **Care for the table.** The fates this game deals out are heavy by design. The fiction can be dark; the table must be safe. See §9.

## 3. Calling for a roll

Roll **only** when both are true: the outcome is **uncertain**, and **failure costs something**. If either is missing, just say what happens.

- No stakes? They succeed. Picking an ordinary lock with all the time in the world is not a roll.
- No uncertainty? Don't roll to "see if" something a competent pirate obviously can do works — narrate it.
- Stakes *and* uncertainty? Set the difficulty (§4), name what's at risk, and let them build their pool: one die always, plus a die for a relevant **skill**, a die for a relevant **domain**, and a mastery die if a **knack** applies (Part Three).

One roll should resolve a meaningful beat of the fiction, not a single muscle-twitch. "Get past the harbour watch" is a roll; "take one more step" is not.

## 4. Setting difficulty

Most actions are **Standard** — roll the pool, keep the highest die. Raise the difficulty when the fiction is against them:

- **Risky** — drop the highest die. The situation is genuinely hard, hostile, or rushed.
- **Dangerous** — drop the two highest dice. The odds are stacked; only the exceptional get through clean.

Set difficulty from the *fiction*, not from a desire to balance the scene. Then let the players lower it: good or excellent **equipment** designed for the task, a clever plan, the right **position**, or help from an ally can each step a Dangerous action down to Risky, or a Risky one to Standard (Parts Two and Nine). Reward preparation by letting it show up here.

## 5. Assigning stress — the craft of this system

When a roll comes up **2–7** (failure, or success-at-a-cost), the character marks stress. Two judgement calls are yours, and they are the heart of running this game.

### Which track?

**Choose the track from what is happening in the fiction, never from the skill that was rolled.** The same failed Brave roll might cost Blood (you took the blow), Reputation (you were seen to flinch), or Luck (you got away with it, but the universe noticed). Ask: *what does this failure actually do to them?*

- **Blood** — the body: wounds, exhaustion, drowning, fire, disease, the shock that lives in the flesh.
- **Tide** — the supernatural got in: ruins, rites, bargains, the deep paying attention. *Use this sparingly and deliberately;* it is the rarest and most frightening track.
- **Reputation** — the name: being bested in front of people who matter, breaking the Code, a story that travels.
- **Luck** — fated misfortune: showing off, pushing a working plan too far, leaving a loose end, tempting fate and getting away with it *for now*.
- **Gold** — the material position: a prize lost, a bribe paid, gear broken, a debt called.

When two tracks both fit, pick the one that makes the better story, or split a larger hit. (Full track behaviour and fallout tables: Part Four.)

### How much?

Set the **stress dice** by severity, anchored to the region's tier (Part Twelve) when you're unsure:

| Dice | When |
|---|---|
| **D4** | Minor trouble; Tier 0–1 default |
| **D6** | Serious; Tier 2–3 default |
| **D8** | Genuinely dangerous; Tier 4 default |
| **D10** | Catastrophic — a cannon broadside, a leviathan's bite |

**Protection** (from class advances, circumstances, or the `Block` tag) reduces the rolled value before it's marked. Equipment can never remove **Tide**, **Luck**, or **Reputation** — remember that when you decide where a hit lands.

## 6. Fallout — making consequence concrete

Every time stress is marked, roll a **D12** against the character's **total** stress across all tracks. Higher than the total: it accumulates, the clock ticks on. Equal or lower: **fallout fires** — on a D12 of 1–6, **Minor** (clear the marked track); on 7–12, **Major** (clear *all* tracks; something has permanently changed).

Your craft here:

- **Pick a fallout that fits the fiction.** The tables in Part Four are a menu, not a mandate. Choose the entry that the moment has earned, or invent one in the same spirit.
- **Write it as a problem with an edge, not a penalty.** Good fallout is a concrete thing in the world — a wound that reopens, a bounty posted, a crewmate who leaves — that *also* has a mechanical bite. "You take −1" is not fallout; "*Festering* — mark D6 Blood each scene until a surgeon sees you" is.
- **Immediate vs. ongoing.** Some fallout resolves now; some rides along for sessions until it's dealt with at a haunt, by a bond, or through story. Track the ongoing ones; they are the texture of a hard life.
- **Major fallout is a turning point.** It wipes the slate (all stress clears) *because* it changes the character. Lean into it: a lost hand, a curse taken, a name destroyed. This is the system breathing — relief and transformation at once.
- **Escalate honestly.** Minor fallout that's ignored is allowed to become Major later (the *Foreboding → Crisis* line is the model). Telegraph, then deliver.

## 7. Pacing the doom

This is the game's reason for existing, and it lives mostly in one track. **Luck does not clear by rest, coin, or carousing — only by its fallout firing** (Part Four). That makes it a one-way ratchet: every audacious, show-off, push-your-luck moment loads it, and sooner or later it goes off. Run it like this:

- **Don't fast-track it.** You never need to manufacture doom. Let players load Luck through their own recklessness; your job is to mark it fairly and let the D12 do its work.
- **When it fires, make it land where it hurts the story,** not at random — the loose end they left, the person they showed off in front of, the corner they cut. The *Collateral*, *The Tab*, and *Fool's Gold* fallouts are your friends.
- **The callings are the long arcs.** Each personal calling points at a **Downfall** (Part Five); the shared **Piracy** calling points at the campaign's two endings. Watch which beats players choose and steer opportunities toward them. A character nearing zenith beats is telling you they're ready for the ending — give it room.
- **A character does not know they have beats.** Never tell a player "this hits your beat." Weave the opportunity into the fiction and let them walk into it. The beat is the *author's* tool; the character just lives.
- **Earn the tragedy.** A Downfall only means something if the liberty before it was real and chosen. Spend real sessions on the good life. Then, when the player reaches for the ending, don't soften it.

## 8. Building the world

You need three kinds of place and one kind of enemy. All use the same statblock grammar (Parts Eleven and Twelve).

- **Landmarks & havens.** Anywhere worth navigating by. A friendly port is a haven — a place to rest, trade, and find people who'd rather not kill you. Give it `DOMAINS / TIER / DEFAULT STRESS / HAUNTS`. Everything that isn't a landmark is protean — don't promise the players a cove will be the same cove next season.
- **Haunts.** The services inside a haven that *remove* stress and fallout, each with a track and a dice cap (*Mother Quay's surgery — D8 Blood*). They are how the crew recovers; price them in trade, downgraded resources, or stress to another track (Part Eleven). Remember Tide and Luck can only be cleared by specific haunts and abilities — make those rare and worth seeking.
- **Journeys.** The passage between two landmarks (Part Eleven), with `DOMAINS / TIER / STRESS / RESISTANCE / EVENTS / CHARTED ROUTE`. At sea, the **Hull** absorbs the sea's work while the crew's own mistakes still land on them (Part Ten). Use the **inland rule** — each step from the shore raises the tier — to make island work two journeys stitched together: a sheltered sea leg, then a land delve with no ship to hide behind.
- **Adversaries.** Stat any threat — a person, a creature, a ship — as Heart stats an NPC: one **Resistance** (their will and capacity to keep fighting), a **protection**, a **difficulty**, the **stress dice** they inflict, any **tags**, and the **resources** they yield when beaten. When their Resistance hits 0, they break the way the fiction wants — strike, flee, founder, surrender, die. The ship adversaries in Part Ten are your worked examples; build people and monsters to the same shape. *(A general bestiary is still on the roadmap — see the [cohesion gap analysis](appendices/cohesion-gap-analysis.md); until then, improvise off the ship statblocks and the tier table.)*

## 9. Session zero

Before the first prize, build the things the whole table shares.

1. **Set the tone and the lines.** Say plainly what this game is: liberty, spectacle, and an ending that comes for everyone. Agree on **safety tools** — lines (never happens) and veils (happens off-screen), and a way for anyone to pause or pull back a scene, no reason owed. The fates here are heavy; the table is not.
2. **Build the crew.** Have everyone make characters together (Part Eight), answering the calling questions that name *each other* out loud so the bonds are tangled before play. Then answer the **Piracy** crew questions as a group (Part Five): the ship and what she cost, the first prize and what went wrong, the home port, and the enemy who already knows your flag.
3. **Define the ship.** Size and quality (Part Ten). A sound working vessel at Hull protection 1 is plenty; let them earn the rest.
4. **Name a home port.** A haven with at least one or two haunts and someone there who is glad — or furious — to see them. This is where they'll heal, refit, and be hunted.
5. **Plant the hooks.** Note each character's Downfall direction, each calling's first beats, and the enemy from the Piracy questions. You now have a season's worth of pressure without writing a plot.

## 10. Beats & advancement at the table

- At the end of each session, players choose **two active beats** from their personal calling or the Piracy pool. Glance at them. They are the players telling you what story they want next.
- Over the coming sessions, **frame situations where those beats can be hit** — without announcing it. A player chasing a Vendetta beat should find a lead; a Fellowship player should be handed a chance to put their body in the way.
- When a beat is hit, the player takes a class **advance** at the matching tier (minor / major / zenith). Advancement *is* the story's shape: minors are the good years, majors the turning points, zeniths the ending. Don't gate advances behind your plot — they belong to the players' beats.
- When a player starts taking **zenith** beats, the ending is beginning. Clear the deck for it. Give that character's last act the weight it has been earning since session one.

---

## First-session checklist

- [ ] Tone and safety tools agreed; lines and veils on the table.
- [ ] Characters built together; calling questions naming each other answered aloud.
- [ ] Piracy crew questions answered; ship (size + quality) and home port defined.
- [ ] One haven with haunts, and one enemy who already knows the flag.
- [ ] A first situation with real stakes — a prize, a debt, a door — and the spotlight handed to a player with the question: *what do you do?*
