# Halfling

Across the Inner Sea, your people are the ones who do the work that holds the great houses up — and in Cheliax, more often than not, they do it in chains. You were born into a household that owned you, or you were born one generation removed from one, and the whole architecture of your early life was built to convince you that this was natural, that your smallness was the same as smallness of soul, that the kindest thing was to be useful and quiet and grateful. You did not believe it. You got out — over a wall, into a hold, off a dock at midnight — and you made for the one corner of the world where no one can legally own a thing that talks, and now you wear your freedom like a knife you keep where they can see it. Halflings are everywhere in the Shackles: cooks, lookouts, pickpockets, quartermasters, the small clever hands that get into the places larger hands cannot. The work is hard and the sea will likely kill you. It is the first life that was ever yours.

## Background Questions

- You escaped a Chelish household — perhaps a noble estate near **Corentyn**, perhaps an Aspis Consortium counting-house — that still has your name in its ledger of property. Who owned you, and how close have they come to dragging you back?
- You were born free in the Shackles to parents who weren't, and you grew up on the stories of the place they ran from. What did they make you promise, and have you kept it?
- You free others now — it's the thing you can't *not* do — and it has made you enemies who profit from the trade in people. Whose operation have you cost the most, and what are they offering for you?

## Names

Halfling names are warm, plain, and easy in the mouth — a given name, a family name that's often a trade or a place, sometimes a slave-name shed and replaced with one chosen in freedom. Examples: *Pim Saltcellar, Lula, Bennet Quick, Mosey, Hessa Freeborn, Corrin Tallows.*

> A halfling born into bondage was often given a single short name by the household. Choosing a second name — a family name, a free name — is the first thing many do on getting out.

## Trinkets

*Roll twice on a d20 and keep both.*

| d20 | Trinket |
|---|---|
| 1 | The household tag or collar you were made to wear, cut through and kept as a reminder |
| 2 | A recipe book in three hands — your grandmother's, your mother's, and now yours |
| 3 | A skeleton key you cut yourself, that opens more than it has any right to |
| 4 | A page torn from a Chelish property-ledger with your old name on it, that you stole on the way out |
| 5 | A set of dice, weighted, that you only use on people who deserve it |
| 6 | A child's shoe, too small for anyone aboard, that you are keeping for a reason you'll tell one day |
| 7 | A list of names — others still inside the house you escaped — that you are working down, one at a time |
| 8 | A cat that runs the rat-lines below decks and answers only to you (name it) |
| 9 | A free-paper, expertly forged, made out to a person who never existed |
| 10 | A spoon worn thin, the only thing of your mother's that made it out with you |
| 11 | A little iron token of Cayden Cailean, patron of the freed, that you rub before anything dangerous |
| 12 | A wanted notice offering a reward "for the return of property," meaning you, that you carry out of spite |
| 13 | A coil of fine cord and a grapple small enough to hide in a fist |
| 14 | A pressed sprig of something that grew in the one window you could see from the scullery |
| 15 | A purse that is never quite as empty as it should be, and you won't say why |
| 16 | A whistle-code, taught by other runaways, that means *get out now* in any port that knows it |
| 17 | A debt owed you by someone you freed, who swore they'd come when you called |
| 18 | A thimble, a needle, and thread fine enough for sailmaker's work or a cut throat |
| 19 | A jar of good salt, because you swore you would never again eat what they fed you |
| 20 | The first coin you ever earned as a free person, that you will starve before you spend |
