# Journeys at Sea

*Journey fundamentals (resistance, boons and banes, abandonment, charted routes) live in Ports & Haunts; the Hull track lives in Hull & the Ship. Nothing here changes land journeys.*

---

## 1. EVERY HAND WORKS THE SHIP

A sea journey is not the navigator's journey. Any character can work toward completing it with any skill the fiction justifies, exactly as on a land delve — the ship simply gives everyone somewhere to stand:

- **Navigate** — the course, the tides, the set of the sails.
- **Brave** — aloft in weather, out on the bowsprit, over the side with a fouled line.
- **Endure** — the long watch, the pumps, the heat of the doldrums.
- **Tend** — nursing rigging, rationing water, keeping the salt sores from festering.
- **Reckon** — the lookout's eye: weather, sails on the horizon, what the birds are doing.
- **Carouse / Command** — a crew that sings is a crew that hauls; a crew that's driven hauls faster, for a while.
- **Hunt / Vanish** — fishing the passage; slipping past the revenue cutter's patrol line unseen.

GM: if one player is rolling the whole passage, you've let the ship become a funnel. Frame events at the places the others stand.

## 2. WHERE THE STRESS LANDS

Heart marks delve failure on the character. The Hull track absorbs *some* of that at sea — not all of it. The split:

- **Failures of seamanship and weather mark Hull** — the gust that splits the topsail, the reef that kisses the keel, the sea that comes over the bow. The sea hits the ship first; the ship is everyone's problem.
- **Failures of judgment, provisioning and attention mark the actor**, exactly as on land — Gold for the supplies you wasted and the long way round; Luck for the wrong turn, the light shown at night, the attention drawn; Blood for what your own carelessness cost your body.

The test: *did the sea do this to the ship, or did you do this to yourselves?* The ship is a buffer against the ocean, not against your mistakes — and the doom engines stay lit because your mistakes still belong to you.

## 3. QUALITY MAKES EASY WATER FREE — DELIBERATELY

Hull protection applies to journey stress as normal, which means a Good or Excellent vessel will shrug most of a D4 passage. **This is intended.** A well-found ship makes Tier 0–1 waters a montage; that is what the money bought, and it is why the work that pays is in Tier 2 and deeper. GM: when the party's quality outgrows the water, don't inflate the dice — move the story to worse water, or send something that doesn't care about copper sheathing.

## 4. THE WATCH ROTATION — REST INVERTS AT SEA

On land, resting on a journey is a bane: you're exposed, and the delve stretches. A crewed ship inverts this. **Routine rest aboard a vessel with a working watch rotation incurs no bane** — people heal, mend and sleep while the journey progresses, because that is what a watch system is for. Healing abilities and Tend rolls function normally under way.

A bane applies, as on land, when the ship must actually *stop*: anchoring in hostile or uncanny water, heaving to in a storm to make repairs, landing a party and waiting on them.

**Small craft are not ships.** A boat without a watch rotation — a longboat, a stolen jolly-boat, a raft of wreckage — keeps the land rules in full: resting is a bane, the sea's stress lands on the people (a boat that size has no meaningful Hull track), and the passage is exactly as miserable as Heart intends. The Castaway's whole backstory lives in this clause.

## 5. THE BEACH IS A SEAM

The inland-tier rule means most island work is two journeys stitched together: a sea journey under the ship's protection, then a land delve without it. Two rules govern the seam:

- **The anchored ship is a refuge, not a haunt.** She is where you sleep, stow, and retreat to — returning to her mid-delve is the normal abandon-a-journey rule — but she provides no refresh beyond ordinary Tend rolls. Floating havens are built, not sailed in.
- **The unattended ship is in play.** While the party is inland, the ship is a legitimate target for consequences — Luck fallout especially. *Your anchorage was spotted. The cable has parted. Something has been aboard.* A crew that leaves undercrew aboard, hides the anchorage, or pays one of the party to stay behind is spending real resources on a real risk, and that is the point.

## SMALLER FLAGS

**Turning back is not symmetric.** The abandon rule (new journey home at half the cleared resistance) assumes the way back resembles the way out. Wind does not. Let the weather gauge modify it: running home before the wind can drop the return a step further; beating back against it can add a bane. Fiction first, then the number.

**Charted routes are seasonal.** A route charted for the trade winds is not charted in storm season, and nothing within reach of the Eye stays charted forever. When a party relies on a route out of season, it counts as uncharted — full resistance — and they should find out the hard way.

**Dismasted is a journey, not a dead end.** Major Hull fallout mid-passage is harsher than any land equivalent, because you cannot limp on foot. When the ship is crippled at sea, convert the remaining passage into a new survival journey — resistance set by the distance left, STRESS stepped up, the jury rig and the water casks as its events. The Sawbone's NO DEAD SHIPS and the Sin Bosun's god both live here.

**Roll cadence.** One journey action represents a day's sailing or one named event, whichever the fiction serves. This keeps Gold consumption, watch fiction and session pacing honest across multi-week passages — a resistance-10 crossing is a week and a half of sea time, not ten minutes of rolling.

**Engagements are events.** A sail on the horizon is a journey event resolved through the engagement ladder; the handoff in both directions (weather gauge in, boon/bane out) is defined in Ships of the Shackles.
