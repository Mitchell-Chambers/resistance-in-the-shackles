# The Shackles — Domains

> *A domain is more than knowledge — it is a life lived in a particular world. It speaks to the contacts you have cultivated, the dangers you can read before they arrive, and the unspoken language you share with everyone who belongs to that same shadow of experience. You either know these waters or you don't.*

---

## What Is a Domain?

Domains are areas of lived experience — environments you know how to read, communities you belong to, subjects you have studied not from books but from necessity. When you have a relevant domain, add one D10 to your pool and pick the highest.

Unlike skills, domains are not strictly about action. They define who you know, what you can identify, and where you are at home. A character with Deepwater can navigate open ocean and read weather. A character with Port has a fence in every dockside market and knows which tavernkeeper keeps her mouth shut.

Domains can also unlock contacts, information, and access that no roll can purchase alone. The GM should allow domain-relevant knowledge to emerge naturally in play — you don't always need to roll when your domain tells you something the fiction has made plain.

---

## Knacks

If you gain a domain a second time, you gain a Knack — a specific, narrow expertise within that domain that allows you to roll with mastery in that particular situation. Each domain lists four suggested Knacks; work with your GM if you want something different that fits your character's history.

Knacks grant mastery (roll an additional D10 and pick the highest), but you cannot gain more than one mastery die per roll regardless of how many Knacks apply.

---

## The Nine Domains

Deepwater · Shallows · Port · Outlaw · Colonial · Arcane · Faith · Ruins · Wilderness

---

## Deepwater

*The open ocean, sea monsters, storms, and the terrible things found far from shore*

You have spent real time out there — past the reefs, past the shipping lanes, out where the water goes black beneath the keel and the horizon is a full circle of nothing. You know what the deep sea does to sailors who aren't ready for it. You know how to read the sky three days ahead. You know what that shape moving under the surface is, and whether to run.

Deepwater characters speak the private language of anyone who has been truly alone at sea. They are comfortable with enormity and silence in ways that unsettle people who have never left sight of land.

### When This Domain Applies

- Navigating through open ocean, fog banks, or strange currents
- Identifying sea monsters, understanding their behaviour, or knowing what bait they won't take
- Surviving storms, water spouts, or the unnatural weather near the Eye of Abendego
- Reading the deep sky for weather, portents, or the positions of stars
- Knowing what killed the sailors found drifting on that abandoned hulk
- Dealing with sailors, fishermen, or anyone else whose life is measured in fathoms

### Knacks

- **Eye-Reader** — You can navigate the Edge of the Eye of Abendego — the permanent hurricane at the heart of the Shackles — without penalty.
- **Depth-Wise** — You know what lurks in the abyss. When you encounter a sea creature or phenomenon, you may ask the GM one question that must be answered honestly.
- **Storm-Seasoned** — You function normally in conditions that impose penalties on others: high seas, howling wind, driving rain.
- **Old Salt** — Your credibility with experienced sailors is absolute. They recognise you as one of their own before you speak.

### Fallout Flavour

> Tide stress accrues when you spend too long in waters that are watching you back. The deep has a long memory.

## Shallows

*Reefs, coastlines, tidal caves, hidden anchorages, and the secrets of island waters*

You know every reef by its colour, every tidal channel by its smell, every hidden cove by the way the pelicans fly above it. You can read a coastline the way a scholar reads a map — faster, and with better information. You know where the wrecks are. You know which beaches have freshwater and which ones have quicksand.

This is local knowledge, intimate and earned through years of getting it wrong. It makes you invaluable to any crew trying to navigate the Shackles' notoriously treacherous island chains, and it makes you someone that port officials can never quite catch.

### When This Domain Applies

- Threading a ship through reef passages, tidal channels, or uncharted shallows
- Finding hidden anchorages, smuggling coves, or pirate bolt-holes along a coastline
- Knowing what's on a given island — who lives there, what they grow, where their fresh water is
- Reading tidal patterns to know when a passage opens or a sandbar disappears
- Identifying wrecks, salvage sites, or sunken obstacles that would gut an unwary ship
- Moving along coastlines on foot, by longboat, or by other small craft without being seen

### Knacks

- **Chart-Mind** — You carry the Shackles coast in your head. You cannot be surprised by the geography — you always have a secondary route.
- **Wreck-Nose** — You can identify the location and approximate contents of a wreck from surface signs: oil sheens, debris, the way the current bends.
- **Reef-Runner** — You can pilot any vessel through shallow reef water at full speed without requiring a roll unless the situation is genuinely exceptional.
- **Beachhead** — Landing parties you lead never suffer surprise on arrival. You pick the right beach every time.

### Fallout Flavour

> Hull stress often finds its cause in the Shallows — the reef that opened the hull, the bar that grounded the ship, the channel that ran dry.

## Port

*Taverns, markets, dockside politics, and the commerce and crime of pirate towns*

You know how port cities actually work — not the surface of it, but the machinery beneath. Who the harbormaster reports to. Which tavernkeeper will hold a message for a week without reading it. Where to find a ship's surgeon at three in the morning. How to disappear into a crowded dockside market before someone's friends find you.

Port covers any haven with a dock and a criminal class, from Port Peril and Bloodcove to a three-shack anchorage on a forgotten island. You understand the social ecosystems of these places: the factions, the debts, the unspoken laws, and how to navigate them without becoming a cautionary tale.

### When This Domain Applies

- Finding a specific person, service, or piece of information in any port town
- Understanding the political landscape of a port — who runs it, who wants to, and who's pretending
- Moving through markets, auctions, or fences to sell plunder or acquire supplies at good prices
- Reading a crowd in a tavern or on a dockside to know if trouble is coming and where from
- Gaining access to a restricted area, a private dock, or a locked warehouse through connections
- Knowing which side of a port dispute to land on, and which debts are owed to whom

### Knacks

- **Dockside Eyes** — You always know if you are being watched in a port, and roughly by whom.
- **Black Market** — You can find a buyer or seller for anything in any port of meaningful size. The price may be uncomfortable, but the connection is real.
- **Known Face** — In any port you have visited before, at least one NPC is genuinely glad to see you and will do you a favour without being asked.
- **Harbour Law** — You know the formal and informal rules of every major Shackles port. You can invoke them to your advantage or to others' disadvantage.

### Fallout Flavour

> Reputation stress is often born in ports, where your name travels faster than your ship and your enemies are always just arrived.

## Outlaw

*Pirate codes and customs, Free Captain politics, smuggling routes, and the law of the sea*

You understand the culture of piracy from the inside: the Pirate Code and what it actually means in practice, the hierarchy of the Free Captains of the Shackles, how ships are crewed and captains are made and unmade. You know what you can get away with and — crucially — what you cannot.

This is not just knowledge of crime but of the entire counter-culture that the Shackles represents: its politics, its grudges, its unwritten rules and violent traditions. You know which captains have earned their Freebooter's Star and which merely bought one. You know the signals that identify a ship as friendly to the Brotherhood of the Coast, and what it means when those signals stop working.

### When This Domain Applies

- Navigating the politics and hierarchy of the Free Captains or the Pirate Council
- Invoking, citing, or exploiting the Pirate Code in a dispute or negotiation
- Understanding the reputation, capabilities, and known ports of any notable pirate crew
- Following or setting up smuggling routes, knowing which waters are watched and which aren't
- Reading whether another crew is friendly, hostile, or playing a complicated game
- Knowing which crimes are acceptable in the Shackles and which ones get you hunted by other pirates

### Knacks

- **Code-Keeper** — You can invoke the Pirate Code with authority. Most Free Captains will pause and hear you out, even if they don't like you.
- **Wanted List** — You know the bounties, habits, and last-known locations of anyone with a significant price on their head in the Shackles.
- **Signal-Wise** — You know every flag, lantern signal, and hull marking used by pirate fleets in the Shackles. Nothing sails without telling you something.
- **Old Grudge** — You know exactly who owes what to whom in the Shackles' tangled web of alliances and blood feuds. This information is power.

### Fallout Flavour

> Reputation stress runs deep here. A pirate who loses standing among their own people has lost the most important thing they had.

## Colonial

*The Chelish Empire, merchant guilds, and the legitimate powers pirates exist to oppose*

You understand the world the Shackles exists in opposition to — the Chelish Empire, Absalom, the great merchant houses of the Inner Sea, their navies, their laws, their courts, and their cultural assumptions. Whether you come from that world, fled it, or studied it as a tactical necessity, you can move within it in ways that most pirates simply cannot.

Colonial knowledge is a double-edged thing in the Shackles. It opens doors — you can impersonate a naval officer, negotiate with a Chelish magistrate, or bluff through a harbour inspection in a way that would leave most Free Captains stumped. But it also marks you, to those paying attention, as someone shaped by a different world.

### When This Domain Applies

- Impersonating a naval officer, merchant official, or representative of a colonial power
- Understanding how colonial bureaucracy, law, and customs enforcement actually function
- Navigating social situations in Chelish-controlled ports, courts, or naval vessels
- Knowing the trade routes, cargo schedules, and patrol patterns of merchant and naval fleets
- Forging, interpreting, or exploiting official documents, letters of marque, or trading licenses
- Evaluating the wealth, politics, and vulnerabilities of a colonial-backed enterprise

### Knacks

- **Naval Protocols** — You can pass as a naval officer without a roll in situations that don't involve extreme scrutiny.
- **Prize Appraiser** — You know exactly what colonial goods are worth, on both the legitimate market and the black one.
- **Paper Trail** — You can create, alter, or interpret official documents convincingly. Your forgeries pass casual inspection automatically.
- **Patrol Routes** — In any colonial-patrolled sea region you know, you can predict where naval vessels will be within a reasonable window of time.

### Fallout Flavour

> Gold stress sometimes comes from underestimating colonial naval firepower. Their ships are newer, their guns are heavier, and they have supply lines.

## Arcane

*Sea magic, cursed objects, witch-pacts, Besmara's blessings, and things older than ships*

Magic in the Shackles is real, practical, and dangerous. It comes from sea witches trading in storm-debts, from Besmara's favour given and violently revoked, from the pre-human ruins beneath the waves, from the ghost-lights that lead sailors to beautiful deaths. You understand this world: not necessarily as a practitioner, but as someone who knows the rules that govern it.

Arcane domain doesn't mean you cast spells. It means you know what the witch on the black-rigged ship is capable of. It means you recognise the ward carved into that lighthouse door before you open it. It means you know which curses can be bargained away and which ones you just have to outrun.

### When This Domain Applies

- Identifying magical effects, cursed objects, or supernatural phenomena at sea or in the Shackles
- Negotiating with magical entities, sea witches, bound spirits, or elemental beings
- Performing or understanding rituals related to maritime magic, weather-working, or sea-pacts
- Recognising the signs of Besmara's favour or wrath — and knowing what they require in response
- Understanding the arcane geography of the Shackles: places of power, magical hazards, ley currents
- Dealing with the supernatural fallout from delving into Ghol-Gan ruins or deeper pre-human sites

### Knacks

- **Ward-Sight** — You can identify magical protections, curses, or active enchantments by observation alone, without touching them.
- **Witch-Tongued** — You can open a negotiation with any magical entity without automatically triggering hostility. They will hear you out.
- **Storm-Debt** — You know how to call in a weather-working, either from a witch-contact or from a standing sea-pact. The cost is up to the GM.
- **Curse-Reader** — When someone is under a curse or magical compulsion, you can identify its nature, its source, and its conditions for removal.

### Fallout Flavour

> Tide stress and Arcane are deeply linked. Magic in the Shackles always costs something, and the sea is always watching to collect.

## Faith

*Gods, their cults, divine favour, omens, and the religious life of the Shackles*

The Shackles runs on faith — not orderly temple faith, but the desperate, pragmatic, deeply personal faith of people who have watched the horizon swallow ships whole and decided that something out there must be listening. Besmara the Pirate Queen, Gozreh of wind and wave, Calistria of vengeance and honey, Cayden Cailean who made a bet with a god and won — these deities have living presences in the Shackles.

You understand religious practice in this context: the omens, the offerings, the rituals that actually matter and those that are just theatre. You know when a priest's blessing is genuine and when it's commerce. You know which temples keep their doors open to criminals and which ones sell confessions to the magistrate.

### When This Domain Applies

- Identifying divine signs, omens, or manifestations and interpreting their meaning
- Understanding the practices, taboos, and internal politics of any Shackles-relevant faith
- Dealing with priests, cultists, flagellants, oracles, and other religious figures
- Appealing to a deity — through prayer, offering, or ritual — in ways that have a genuine chance of being heard
- Reading whether a location is under divine favour or disfavour, blessed or cursed
- Moving through the social world of temples, shrines, pilgrimage routes, and holy sites

### Knacks

- **Besmara's Eye** — You can read Besmara's favour toward a plan or a vessel. She will not always be pleased, but she will always be honest.
- **Rite of Safe Harbour** — You know the prayers and offerings that invoke the hospitality protections of major faiths. Attacking you on holy ground becomes a divine transgression.
- **Omen-Wise** — You recognise significant omens when they appear and can interpret their likely meaning without a roll.
- **Temple Connections** — In any port with a functioning temple district, you have at least one contact who owes the faith something — and therefore you something.

### Fallout Flavour

> Tide stress comes from witnessing the gods act directly. There is something that breaks in a person when they realise the divine is real and paying attention.

## Ruins

*Sunken cities, Ghol-Gan remnants, pre-human sites, and what's buried beneath the Shackles*

The Shackles is built on top of things that were here long before humanity arrived. The cyclops empire of Ghol-Gan left ruins across every island, some above water and many below. Older things still lurk in the deepest trenches and beneath certain islands that have never been properly settled, for reasons that become clear quickly.

You know how to read these places: where the exits are, what the carvings mean, how to distinguish a simple collapsed ceiling from a deliberate trap that's been waiting since before the Earthfall. You know which ruins are worth the risk and which ones are best left sealed. You are probably the only person in your crew who reads Cyclops.

### When This Domain Applies

- Delving into ancient ruins, sunken structures, or pre-human sites in and around the Shackles
- Reading carvings, sigils, or architecture from Ghol-Gan or older civilisations
- Identifying traps, structural dangers, or supernatural guardians associated with ancient sites
- Locating and extracting valuable artefacts, charts, or knowledge from dangerous ruins
- Understanding the historical context of a ruin and what it was originally built to do
- Dealing with the undead, constructs, or entities left behind as guardians or remnants

### Knacks

- **Ghol-Gan Script** — You can read Cyclops inscriptions and architectural records. The ruins will tell you their secrets if you know how to listen.
- **Trap-Sense** — You never trigger a physical trap without an opportunity to notice it first. The GM will always give you a beat.
- **Relic Appraiser** — You can identify the purpose, value, and likely dangers of any pre-Earthfall artefact on sight.
- **Cartographer of the Deep** — Once you have explored a ruin, you can produce an accurate map of it from memory. These maps are extraordinarily valuable.

### Fallout Flavour

> Tide stress is endemic to Ruins. These places were saturated with power when they were built, and that power hasn't gone anywhere — it has simply become unfocused and hungry.

## Wilderness

*Jungle interiors, island ecosystems, survival, and the living things that want to kill you*

Most pirates stay close to the coast. You have been into the interior of islands that don't appear on any charts, found your way out again, and learned something useful in the process. You know which fruit to eat and which ones will end your expedition in an hour. You know how to follow fresh water uphill to its source. You know the signs that something large is tracking you.

The Shackles' islands range from tropical paradises to nightmarish jungle ecosystems that seem actively hostile to human life. Wilderness domain means you have met the latter on their own terms and formed a working relationship. You are not a city person who got lost. You are someone the jungle has decided to tolerate.

### When This Domain Applies

- Surviving in island interiors, jungles, swamps, or other hostile natural environments
- Tracking creatures or people through wilderness terrain
- Identifying plants, animals, fungi, and other natural elements — for food, medicine, or poison
- Finding fresh water, shelter, or natural resources in an apparently barren or hostile environment
- Moving through dense terrain quickly and quietly, or reading sign that others have passed
- Dealing with the megafauna, predators, and bizarre creatures that inhabit the Shackles' wilder islands

### Knacks

- **Island-Wise** — On any island you have spent more than a day on, you always know roughly where you are, where the water is, and what is dangerous nearby.
- **Naturalist** — You can identify any plant or creature in the Shackles region on sight and know its properties: toxic, edible, medicinal, or territorial.
- **Tracker** — You can follow any trail that exists to follow. In difficult conditions, you roll without penalty on tracking rolls.
- **Jungle Friend** — Animals do not attack you without provocation. Creatures that would normally be dangerous or skittish treat you as a known entity.

### Fallout Flavour

> Blood stress comes freely from the Wilderness. The jungle does not hate you. It is simply hungry, and you are warm.
