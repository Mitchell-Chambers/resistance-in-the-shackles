# The Shackles

## Callings

*A Homebrew Supplement · Heart: The City Beneath / Wildsea*

## What Is a Calling?

Your calling is your reason for sailing. It is the obsession that keeps you from a quiet life ashore — the thing you are willing to die, go mad, or lose everything in pursuit of. You are not a normal person. Normal people do not go to sea. Normal people do not follow a legend to the edge of the Eye of Abendego or burn a Chelish frigate for the look on its captain's face.

A calling is not a class, a background, or a profession. It is closer to a wound that has organised itself into a direction. Two characters with the same class can have entirely different callings, and the same calling can be played in completely different ways. The calling tells you what your character cannot stop wanting. Everything else follows from that.

## How Callings Work

Each calling grants a core ability, a set of questions to answer at character creation, and a list of beats. Beats are goals, milestones, and scenes that your character moves toward over the course of the campaign. At the end of each session, choose two beats and tell the GM. The GM will weave opportunities to hit them into the next session; you push your character toward them. When you hit a beat, take an advance from your class at the appropriate level.

Minor beats can usually be achieved in a single session. Major beats take two or three. Zenith beats signal the beginning of the end — one or two sessions remain before your character's story concludes. You can only have two beats active at any time and cannot hit more than one in any given situation.

## What You Carry

Each calling includes a table of ten objects. Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling. These objects are not equipment in the mechanical sense — they do not grant bonuses or occupy inventory slots. They are evidence. Evidence of the life you were living before the campaign began, of the obsession that brought you here, of the thing you cannot put down even when it would be sensible to do so. They should invite questions. You do not have to answer all of them at character creation.

## The Shared Calling

Every character in the Shackles holds two callings simultaneously: Piracy, which is shared by everyone and covers the beats of the life itself, and a personal calling that covers the specific obsession that makes this character who they are. At the end of each session, choose two active beats from either list — the pools are combined and there is no requirement to draw from both.

## The Downfall Beat

Every calling carries a Downfall zenith beat. It is always listed last. It is never required. Selecting it signals that you are ready for your character's story to end not in triumph or transcendence, but in the specific collapse that their obsession made inevitable. Not all pirate stories end in glory. The Downfall beat exists to honour the ones that don't.

## The Callings

*Piracy (Shared) · Hunger · Monument · Vendetta · Freedom · Cursed Tides · Legacy · Fellowship*

## Hunger

*The insatiable need to acquire, reach, and possess — treasure, discovery, the next horizon*

The wanting never stops. You have learned to call it different things at different times — treasure, discovery, the next horizon, the cargo hold of the ship you have not taken yet. But it is the same hunger wearing different clothes. You are not satisfied with what you have. You are not satisfied with what you can see from where you stand.

You will not be satisfied. The question is only what you spend in the pursuit of the next thing, and whether it was worth it. Some Hunger characters are treasure hunters, defined by what they can hold. Others are explorers, defined by where they can stand. Both are expressions of the same pathology: the horizon always promises something better than what you are carrying.

> **CORE ABILITY** Once per session, you may declare that you know a rumour about the location or nature of something you are hunting — a treasure, a place, a passage, a secret. The GM must confirm whether it is true or false.

### Character Questions

-   *What was the first thing you wanted so badly it changed you — and did you get it?*

-   *Name the thing you are currently hunting. Is it something you can hold, or something you can only stand in?*

-   *What have you already sacrificed to the wanting, and does it still seem like a fair trade?*

-   *Who else knows what you are hunting, and are they ahead of you or behind you?*

### Minor Beats

-   Acquire something that rightfully belongs to another PC or allied NPC, and justify it to yourself.

-   Refuse payment you desperately need, on principle or out of pride.

-   Be recognised and threatened by someone you previously robbed or left behind.

-   Get caught in the act of taking something — from a person, a place, or a situation that was not yours to take from.

-   Take Minor Loot fallout.

-   Spend a resource you have been hoarding at a critical moment.

-   Find something objectively worthless that you irrationally refuse to discard.

-   Outbid, swindle, or fast-talk a rival out of something they were about to acquire.

-   Make a deal you know you cannot honour, and shake on it anyway.

-   Describe the single thing you are ultimately hunting — a treasure, a place, a passage — and share its history with another PC.

-   Go without comfort or safety rather than slow down the pursuit.

-   Barter something you took from one person to another who recognises where it came from.

-   Let the hunger lead you into a clearly dangerous situation without a plan.

-   Acquire an NPC bond who can help you get closer: a fence, a cartographer, a navigator who knows waters others will not sail.

-   Arrive somewhere new and spend your first moments not on safety, but on looking.

-   Discover that something you have been carrying is far more significant than you realised — materially, historically, or geographically.

-   Have your belongings, charts, or cargo inspected and temporarily confiscated by a port authority.

-   Convince another PC to go somewhere dangerous by promising them a share — of the gold, the discovery, the view.

-   Pass up a chance to do the right thing because the right thing means stopping.

-   Set foot somewhere no member of your crew has been before, and take a moment to mark it.

-   Follow a rumour about an uncharted island, a lost wreck, or a hidden passage — even knowing it is probably false.

-   Return to somewhere you have been before and find it changed beyond recognition.

### Major Beats

-   Successfully take something — a prize, a place, a secret — that significant opposition tried to keep from you.

-   Betray an ally for a share of what you were hunting — and face lasting consequences for it.

-   Lose your most prized possession or your most coveted chart: stolen, destroyed, lost to the sea, or taken as debt.

-   Discover that the treasure or the place you have been hunting is cursed, wrong, or worse than you imagined — and choose to press on anyway.

-   Take Major Loot fallout.

-   Sell or surrender something irreplaceable to secure knowledge of what lies ahead.

-   Reach somewhere that no reliable chart shows — and have it be real, dangerous, and nothing like what you expected.

-   Come into possession of something so significant that factions will kill to reclaim or suppress it.

-   Discover that the true value of what you found is not material — it is historical, cartographic, spiritual, or deeply personal — and wrestle with whether that is enough.

-   Help another PC reach something they have been hunting, at genuine cost to yourself.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Prize.*** Reach the thing you have been hunting — the treasure, the place, the passage, the coast — and choose what it costs you to claim it, record it, or simply stand in it.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Die for it, or lose everything chasing it, and in the final moment decide whether the wanting was ever really about the thing at all.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **HUNGER vs. MONUMENT** *Both involve pursuing something with obsessive focus. The difference is direction: Hunger takes — it consumes prizes, places, and experiences and moves immediately toward the next one. Monument builds — it constructs something intended to outlast the builder. A Hunger character asks what is over the next horizon. A Monument character asks what they are leaving behind.*
>
> **HUNGER vs. LEGACY** *Legacy characters are also defined by something precious — but it is inherited, not hunted. A Legacy character begins with something they did not earn and must decide what to do with it. A Hunger character begins with nothing and goes to take it. If you want a character shaped by wanting something they were once denied, Hunger is the right frame. If you want a character shaped by something already theirs, Legacy is closer.*
>
> **HUNGER vs. CURSED TIDES** *Both callings can produce characters who sail toward the unknown. The difference is agency: a Hunger character chooses what they pursue. A Cursed Tides character is pulled. The horizon calls a Hunger character because they have decided it does. It calls a Cursed Tides character whether they want it to or not.*
>
> **HUNGER vs. VENDETTA** *Both callings can begin with the same event — something was taken, and the character goes after it. The difference is what they are actually following. A Hunger character follows the thing. If the treasure passes from the person who stole it to someone else, a Hunger character follows the treasure. A Vendetta character follows the person. If the thing is lost, destroyed, or irrelevant, the Vendetta does not end — because it was never about the thing. It was about the account that needs settling. Hunger is satisfied by acquisition. Vendetta is satisfied only by confrontation.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A hand-drawn chart of a place that does not appear on any official map, annotated in three different hands.
2. A small brass compass that does not point north — you have not yet worked out what it points at.
3. A coin from a mint that no longer exists, which you took from the first thing you ever stole.
4. A folded letter of provenance for an item you do not own yet.
5. A set of lockpicks in a case worn smooth with daily handling.
6. A list of everyone who has tried to acquire the same thing you are after, with notes on what happened to each of them.
7. A dried flower pressed inside a navigation journal, marking a specific page — the journal itself is gone.
8. A spyglass with a crack in the lens that you refuse to replace because it was your first.
9. A small clay jar sealed with wax, contents unknown, taken from a place you have not told anyone about.
10. Three overlapping maps of the same stretch of coastline, none of which agree with each other.

## Monument

*The patient construction of legend, power, and something that will stand after you are gone*

Brick by brick. That is how you think about it, even if you would never say so aloud. Every ship that turns at your flag is a brick. Every story told about you in a dockside tavern is a brick. Every captain who owes you a favour, every port where your name opens a door, every enemy who thinks twice — bricks, all of them.

You are building something. Most pirates take. You are making. The question that keeps you awake is not whether you will finish it. It is whether what you are building deserves the name you are already giving it in your quietest moments: monument.

> **CORE ABILITY** When you introduce yourself to an NPC by your true name and reputation, the GM must tell you honestly how that name lands — whether it is known, feared, mocked, or forgotten. Once per session, you may also declare that you are making a move toward consolidating something — a port, a route, a crew's loyalty, a council vote — and ask the GM what the single most significant obstacle to that consolidation currently is. The GM must answer honestly.

### Character Questions

-   *What are you building, and what do you call it when you describe it to yourself in honest moments?*

-   *What have you already laid down as foundation — and is it solid, or are there cracks you are hoping no one notices?*

-   *Who holds something load-bearing in what you are constructing — a person whose loyalty or fear is structural — and what happens if you lose them?*

-   *What would make everything you have built meaningless — what single thing, if it happened, would prove the monument was built on sand?*

### Minor Beats

-   Be mistaken for something more finished, more powerful, or more legitimate than you currently are.

-   Humiliate a rival or enemy publicly — remove a brick from their structure and add it to yours.

-   Take Minor Reputation fallout when something you built fails visibly in front of people who were watching.

-   Have a story told about you — correctly or not — by an NPC who does not know you are present; note whether it is the story you would have chosen.

-   Refuse a sensible tactical retreat because retreat would mean undoing something you have already built.

-   Commit an act of deliberate construction — a gesture, a favour, a flag, a declaration — that is about the long game rather than the immediate situation.

-   Hear your name used as a reference point by someone who has never met you: a benchmark, a warning, a standard.

-   Demand terms from an enemy who has the upper hand, and frame those terms as a structural offer rather than a desperate plea.

-   Lay a foundation in a port, faction, or relationship — a small piece of influence you intend to build on.

-   Take credit, truthfully or not, for something another pirate did — and add it to the structure.

-   Deliver a speech, threat, or declaration designed not for the moment but for the retelling.

-   Have a ship change course because of your flag alone — and recognise it as evidence the structure is holding.

-   Perform an act of calculated mercy specifically because the story of your mercy is load-bearing.

-   Commission, cultivate, or correct a piece of your own myth — a song, a rumour, a version of events.

-   Learn that a piece of what you have built has spread somewhere you did not place it — the structure is growing on its own.

-   Shore up someone else's position at genuine cost to your own — because their stability is part of your foundation.

-   Suffer a public blow to your Reputation track — and identify exactly which brick just came loose.

-   Acquire leverage over an NPC in a position of power: a debt, a secret, a favour that makes them structurally yours.

-   Back a candidate, cause, or faction in a dispute — openly or quietly — because their outcome matters to what you are making.

-   Identify the single obstacle most threatening to your structure — a person, a rival, a piece of missing legitimacy — and name it aloud to another PC.

### Major Beats

-   Have your name formally entered into the record of an enemy power — a Wanted notice, a Chelish naval mandate, an Aspis dossier — as evidence that what you are building has been noticed.

-   Secure a formal acknowledgement of your standing from the Free Captains, the Pirate Council, or an institution with real authority — a brick of legitimacy laid into something built from fear and story alone.

-   Seize control of something meaningful — a port berth, a trade route, a faction's allegiance — and hold it against active opposition.

-   Inspire someone — a crew member, a port dweller, a rival — to begin building something of their own because of what they saw in you.

-   Suffer a catastrophic, public failure — a piece of the structure collapses in full view — and make the decision about whether to rebuild or let it fall.

-   Take Major Reputation fallout as the cost of overbuilding — reaching further than your foundation can currently support.

-   Discover that someone you trusted has been working against the structure — undermining your authority, your reputation, or your alliances — for longer than you knew.

-   Make an enemy of someone previously powerful enough to ignore you — because you are no longer beneath their notice.

-   Be offered legitimacy — a letter of marque, a pardon, a seat on a council — and choose whether it is a brick or a trap.

-   Be forced to choose between the legend and the power — the act that makes the myth versus the act that consolidates the structure.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Monument.*** What you built stands. Step back from it — into legend, retirement, or death — and watch it hold without you.

-   ***The Throne.*** Claim the seat the structure was always pointing toward — the captaincy, the council chair, the free port under your flag — and discover what it costs to actually sit in it.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Watch it come down — the name, the power, the people who believed in what you were making — and in the wreckage, decide whether building it was worth the life it took.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **MONUMENT vs. HUNGER** *Both are acquisitive, but differently. Hunger takes things and moves on. Monument takes things and builds with them. A Hunger character will cross the world for a single artefact and care nothing for what comes after. A Monument character acquires the same artefact because of what it represents to the structure — a brick, not a prize.*
>
> **MONUMENT vs. LEGACY** *Legacy inherits. Monument constructs. A Legacy character begins the campaign already carrying something of weight. A Monument character begins with nothing and builds toward weight. Both callings are interested in what endures beyond a single life — but Legacy asks what you do with something given, while Monument asks what you do with nothing.*
>
> **MONUMENT vs. FELLOWSHIP** *Both callings produce characters who care about their standing in a community. Monument cares about that standing as material — as bricks in a structure. Fellowship cares about it as relationship — as people. A Monument character keeps allies because they are useful to the structure. A Fellowship character keeps allies because they are irreplaceable people. Both callings can coexist in the same character, but they will create genuine internal tension.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A ship's manifest bearing your name as owner of a vessel you do not yet possess.
2. A broadsheet carrying a story about you — the details are wrong in three specific ways, none of which you have corrected.
3. A collection of signed letters of debt from captains who owe you favours, bundled and tied with red cord.
4. A custom seal and wax, used to mark correspondence as coming from your hand.
5. A flag of your own design, never yet flown, waiting for the ship worthy of it.
6. A ledger of every significant thing you have done, kept in your own hand, with the date and the witnesses.
7. A miniature portrait of yourself commissioned at significant expense, which does not quite look like you.
8. A copy of the Pirate Code with your own annotations in the margins — corrections, additions, objections.
9. The key to a lock you have not yet found, given to you by someone who said you would know what it opened when the time came.
10. A half-finished proclamation drafted in your own hand, unsigned, declaring something you are not yet ready to claim.

## Vendetta

*The obsession with a single target and the ruin that single-mindedness brings*

Somebody took something from you that cannot be replaced. A ship. A crew. A family. Your freedom. You did not choose piracy so much as it chose you, and every sail you run down is practice for the one that matters. The sea is not your home. It is the distance between you and what you owe.

You will not stop until you have looked them in the eye. The problem with that — the one you already know, deep down — is that you have no idea what comes after.

> **CORE ABILITY** Name your target at character creation — a person, an institution, or a fleet. When you take actions that directly advance your vendetta, you may remove one stress from your Nerve track.

### Character Questions

-   *What did they take from you?*

-   *Have you seen them since?*

-   *What will you do when it is finally over?*

-   *What have you become in the pursuit of this — and is that person someone you would have recognised before it started?*

### Minor Beats

-   Encounter someone who knows your target personally — a crewmember, a lover, a debtor.

-   Damage something that matters to your target: property, reputation, a loyal subordinate.

-   Be offered a chance at easy peace, settlement, or forgiveness — and refuse it.

-   Find evidence that your target is more complicated than you believed, and press on regardless.

-   Put another PC at genuine risk because of your obsession with the vendetta.

-   Take Minor Blood fallout from pressing forward when withdrawal would have been wiser.

-   Learn something new about what your target did, and have it make things worse.

-   Encounter a stranger who suffered at the same hands, and handle it badly.

-   Lie to an ally about what you are doing and why.

-   Acquire an NPC bond who has useful intelligence on your target's movements or allies.

-   Destroy, deface, or claim something of your target's as a symbolic act.

-   Follow a lead on your target into a place or situation that puts the whole crew in danger.

-   Describe, in full, what was taken from you — to another PC, to an NPC, or to yourself alone at sea.

-   Let hatred steer you past a better decision in a moment where you had a genuine choice.

-   Do something your target would recognise as a message, whether or not they receive it.

-   Receive word that your target knows you are coming.

-   Help someone else with their own grievance in hopes of gaining intelligence or allies for yours.

-   Spend a significant resource — coin, goodwill, or opportunity — chasing a lead that goes cold.

### Major Beats

-   Destroy a major asset of your target: a flagship, a stronghold, a reputation, a trusted lieutenant.

-   Be captured, cornered, or held by your target or their agents.

-   Learn something about your target that genuinely complicates your hatred — and sit with it.

-   Cause a PC or significant NPC genuine, lasting harm through your single-minded pursuit of revenge.

-   Take Major Blood fallout while directly pursuing your vendetta.

-   Be given an opportunity to end it without direct confrontation — and choose whether to take it.

-   Discover that someone close to you also has a history with your target, and that their grief is different from yours.

-   Your target makes a direct move against you, your crew, or something you value, outside the context of you hunting them.

-   Achieve a partial victory against your target — something real, but not enough.

-   Ally yourself with someone you despise because they share a common enemy.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Reckoning.*** Face your target directly; the confrontation arrives, and you make a choice about what you do with it.

-   ***The Inheritance.*** Pass the vendetta to someone else — willingly or not — and live with what that means for who you were.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Realise the vendetta has become you — and let it finish what it started, or burn it out and find out who is left in the ash.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **VENDETTA vs. HUNGER** *Both callings can begin with the same event — something was taken, and the character gives chase. The difference is what the chase is actually for. A Vendetta character is following a person — a specific, named wrong that needs a specific answer. A Hunger character is following a thing, a place, or an experience — and if the person who has it dies tomorrow, the Hunger simply moves to whoever has it next. Ask the player: if your target surrendered the thing they took from you but walked away free, would that be enough? If yes, the calling is Hunger. If the answer is no — if what you need is to look them in the eye — the calling is Vendetta.*
>
> **VENDETTA vs. FREEDOM** *Both callings can begin with the same origin — someone was wronged, enslaved, or destroyed. The difference is where they point. Vendetta points at a specific target. Freedom points at a system. A Vendetta character wants one person or institution to suffer. A Freedom character wants the conditions that produced the harm to be dismantled. Both are valid responses to the same injury.*
>
> **VENDETTA vs. CURSED TIDES** *Both callings produce characters driven by something they cannot fully control. But a Vendetta character is driven by a specific, named grievance — something that happened and a specific wrong they intend to answer. A Cursed Tides character is driven by something diffuse, inhuman, and impossible to aim. Vendetta is focused. Cursed Tides is consumed.*
>
> **VENDETTA vs. LEGACY** *A Vendetta character is defined by what was done to them. A Legacy character is defined by what was done before them. Both involve a past that presses on the present, but Vendetta is active — the character is in pursuit — while Legacy is more ambivalent. A Legacy character may or may not want to honour what came before. A Vendetta character has no ambivalence about the direction of travel.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A piece of your target's property — taken, not given — that you carry as proof of what was done and what you intend.
2. A scar or mark on your body that your target gave you, which you have refused to have properly treated.
3. A list of your target's known allies, associates, and ports of call, updated in your own hand whenever new information arrives.
4. A loaded pistol kept specifically for this purpose, never fired at anyone else, cleaned and maintained obsessively.
5. A letter you wrote to your target and never sent, explaining everything.
6. The name of a ship — your target's — carved into a piece of driftwood you carry as a ward and a promise.
7. A court document, a bill of sale, or an official record proving the wrong that was done, which no court will ever hear.
8. A portrait or description of your target so detailed you could identify them in any port, in any disguise.
9. A small object that belonged to what was taken from you — the only piece of it that survived.
10. A counting device of some kind — notches on a belt, knots in a cord — tracking how long you have been in pursuit.

## Freedom

*The need to burn every cage — your own, and everyone else's*

You were owned once. Maybe legally — a Chelish thrall, an indentured navigator, a press-ganged deckhand. Maybe less formally — a debtor, a spouse, a conscript. The mechanism doesn't matter. What matters is that you understand, in your body, what it means to have your choices removed.

The sea is the only place where no one can put walls around you, and you will burn every cage you find. Some Freedom characters do this quietly, one lock at a time. Others are conflagrations. Either way, the work is the same: every cage you see is personal. Every chain is yours to break.

> **CORE ABILITY** You cannot be coerced into a course of action you have explicitly refused. When an NPC attempts to threaten, blackmail, or magically compel you, you automatically succeed on resisting — but the GM may still route consequences through other channels.

### Character Questions

-   *Who held the leash?*

-   *How did you get free?*

-   *What do you do when you see someone else in chains?*

-   *What does freedom actually look like to you — not the absence of chains, but the thing you are sailing toward?*

### Minor Beats

-   Free a prisoner, thrall, conscript, or indentured person, even at cost to yourself.

-   Refuse a direct order from a captain, patron, or authority figure you genuinely respect.

-   Take Minor Nerve fallout from refusing to run when withdrawal would have been wiser.

-   Destroy a legal or binding document — a contract, letter of marque, bill of sale, writ of ownership.

-   Be briefly recaptured, bound, imprisoned, or held against your will.

-   Encounter someone who has chosen their cage, and feel contempt, pity, or uncomfortable recognition.

-   Describe your captivity to another PC, in full, for the first time.

-   Acquire an NPC bond who was freed by or alongside you.

-   Refuse the safety of a port, haven, or arrangement that comes with conditions attached.

-   Commit an act of solidarity with people you have no obligation to.

-   Pass up coin or opportunity rather than work under someone else's authority.

-   Speak openly and at risk about the conditions you escaped, in front of people who benefit from those conditions.

-   Help another PC refuse something they had resigned themselves to accepting.

-   Instigate or enable a small mutiny, work stoppage, or act of collective defiance aboard any vessel.

-   Do something you were explicitly forbidden from doing, not out of selfishness but principle.

-   Turn down shelter, hospitality, or comfort because accepting it would mean owing something.

-   Recognise your own patterns of captivity in a place or situation that looks like freedom.

-   Come to the defence of someone being subjected to a cruelty you know personally.

### Major Beats

-   Liberate a vessel from its owners and formally hand it to its crew.

-   Burn, destroy, or dismantle a slaving operation, press-gang network, or system of indentured servitude.

-   Be recaptured and held long enough for it to cost you something real — and fight your way back out.

-   Refuse an alliance, offer, or opportunity that would require compromising your principles in ways you cannot walk back.

-   Take Major Nerve fallout from the weight of what freedom has cost, and what it still demands.

-   Witness the liberation of many people at once, and experience how complicated that is.

-   Free someone who later uses their freedom in a way you find unforgivable.

-   Confront the person or institution that originally owned or controlled you.

-   Be accused — not unfairly — of imposing your idea of freedom on someone who wanted a different choice.

-   Sacrifice a position of genuine power or influence rather than use it to control others.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Horizon.*** Sail somewhere no chart has ever reached, beyond jurisdiction, beyond debt, beyond anyone's claim on you.

-   ***The Fire.*** Bring down the thing that owned you — the institution, the system, the person — and stand in what is left.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Die unchained — or discover, too late, that the cage you have been fleeing was one you carried with you all along.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **FREEDOM vs. VENDETTA** *Both can begin in the same place — a wrong done, a person destroyed or stolen. Freedom points at systems; Vendetta points at targets. A Freedom character may have a nemesis, but their obsession is with the structure that made that nemesis possible, not the nemesis themselves. If the target died tomorrow, a Vendetta character would grieve the loss of purpose. A Freedom character would keep working.*
>
> **FREEDOM vs. FELLOWSHIP** *Both callings can produce characters who fight for others. Freedom does so from principle — the belief that captivity is wrong, universally. Fellowship does so from love — these specific people are worth protecting. A Freedom character will burn a slaving operation run by someone they have never met. A Fellowship character's calculus is always about the crew in front of them. Both are generous callings. They are generous differently.*
>
> **FREEDOM vs. CURSED TIDES** *Freedom characters choose their direction. Cursed Tides characters are pulled in one whether they choose it or not. Both callings can produce characters who seem to be following an inexorable path — but Freedom's path is an active ethical commitment, while Cursed Tides' path is something done to the character from the outside.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A broken manacle — one half of a pair — worn on a cord around your neck.
2. A forged document granting you freedom, citizenship, or legitimacy in a nation that would never issue it legitimately.
3. The brand or mark of whoever owned you, covered or altered by your own hand into something that belongs to you now.
4. A small book of songs, prayers, or words that sustained you during captivity, written in handwriting that is not yours.
5. A list of names — people still in the situation you escaped — that you carry until you can do something about each one.
6. A key that fits no lock you have ever found, taken from the person who held you.
7. An empty contract — unsigned, but filled out with terms for your own freedom — that you carry as a reminder of what freedom actually costs.
8. A piece of clothing, a tool, or an object you were not allowed to have, kept now as proof that no one gets to tell you that anymore.
9. A hand-drawn map of the place you were held, in extraordinary detail, made from memory after you left.
10. A small folded piece of paper on which someone once wrote your name — the first time anyone asked what your name was rather than what your number was.

## Cursed Tides

*The sea has claimed you — or something beneath it has — and the calling has no bottom*

The sea spoke to you. Or something beneath it did. You did not ask for this — the dreams, the compulsions, the way certain stretches of water feel like they are watching you. Maybe it was Besmara's hand. Maybe it was something older, something that predates the gods.

You sail because the sea calls you forward, and because stopping feels like drowning from the inside. Other pirates have reasons. You have a pull. The distinction matters less than you would like it to.

> **CORE ABILITY** Once per session, you may ask the GM what the sea, the weather, or the deep water wants from you right now. The GM must answer honestly.

### Character Questions

-   *When did you first feel it?*

-   *What happens if you stay too long on land?*

-   *What do you think is waiting for you at the end?*

-   *What does the call feel like when it is strong — describe it as a physical sensation, a sound, or a presence?*

> *The Arcane domain is a natural complement for characters drawn to this Calling, reflecting knowledge of sea magic, Besmaran ritual, and the deep-water forces that press upon you. This is a suggestion, not a rule.*

### Minor Beats

-   Take Minor Hex fallout from a vision, dream, or compulsion that arrives at the worst possible moment.

-   Be warned of danger by something inexplicable — and have the warning be accurate.

-   Consume or come into contact with something of the deep: salt-preserved relics, sea-beast flesh, water from a drowned ruin.

-   Damage a port, haven, or anchorage by letting something in that should have stayed out.

-   Terrify or fascinate an NPC with the things you say and the way you say them.

-   Perform a rite at a place where the ocean feels wrong — a reef, a becalmed stretch, a site of old wreckage.

-   Communicate with something beneath the surface; describe what it says and what you give in return.

-   Name the thing that calls to you — give it a word, a symbol, or a sound.

-   Undergo a trance or fugue state that lasts for hours and leaves you somewhere you do not remember sailing to.

-   Refuse to kill something of the deep when killing it would have been easier.

-   See something from your dreams appear in the waking world — on the water, on another person's face, in the sky.

-   Build a small shrine or offering at sea — floated out on a current, sunk deliberately, fixed to your mast.

-   Convince your crew to sail a course that only you believe is right.

-   Receive insight or a warning from a priest of Besmara, a deep-water oracle, or something stranger still.

-   Act against your own interests because the tide or the current or the pull of the thing demanded it.

-   Gain a physical mark or change — a stain, a wound that does not heal right, a feature that shifts with the moon.

-   Find evidence that others have been called in the same way, and that most of them ended badly.

-   Shelter or protect someone else who has been touched by the deep, at personal risk.

### Major Beats

-   Perform a rite at a deep-sea landmark, sunken ruin, or forgotten sacred site — and receive something in return.

-   Sacrifice something you love to the tide — an object, a relationship, a future.

-   Take Major Hex fallout from the sea's claim pressing closer.

-   Show the truth of what calls you to an outsider, and watch them break, believe, or run.

-   One of your bonds takes significant fallout directly because of your connection to the deep.

-   Establish contact with something of the deep that recognises you — and treats you accordingly.

-   Lead your crew to a place the charts do not show, and have it be real.

-   Perform an act directly requested by the thing that calls you, and live with what that means.

-   Destroy a haven, safe harbour, or protected anchorage because the tide required it.

-   Discover another person who received the same call — and learn that they answered it differently.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Answer.*** Answer the call completely: go beneath the waves, and do not return as anything the world would recognise.

-   ***The Severance.*** Refuse the sea's final claim — rip yourself free of it, sever what bound you, and face the silence where the call used to be.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Let the tide have what it always said it would take — your ship, your crew, yourself — and go down with something like peace.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **CURSED TIDES vs. HUNGER** *Both callings can pull a character toward the unknown. Hunger is chosen — the character has decided to pursue this thing. Cursed Tides is inflicted. A Hunger character can, in principle, stop. A Cursed Tides character cannot. The horizon is always there whether they sail toward it or not.*
>
> **CURSED TIDES vs. LEGACY** *Both callings involve a force outside the character that shapes their path — Legacy through bloodline, Cursed Tides through supernatural compulsion. The difference is relationship. A Legacy character can choose to reject, honour, or transform what they inherited. A Cursed Tides character cannot negotiate with what calls them. Legacy offers a choice. Cursed Tides offers only a direction.*
>
> **CURSED TIDES vs. FREEDOM** *At first glance these callings seem opposed — one is about being pulled, one is about refusing to be pulled. In practice they produce a particular kind of internal conflict: a Freedom character touched by Cursed Tides is someone who has escaped one claim only to find they are already held by another. If you want that tension in a single character, these two callings in the same party will generate it constantly.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A piece of coral that grew around your wrist while you were sleeping, which you have cut back but cannot fully remove.
2. A journal of visions that is almost entirely water-damaged — the few legible pages are disturbing.
3. A fragment of hull timber from a ship that sank with all hands, which sometimes feels warm.
4. A small bottle of water taken from a specific stretch of ocean that glows faintly on nights with no moon.
5. A tattoo of a creature you have seen only in dreams, placed there by a Besmaran priest who said you would understand it eventually.
6. A piece of paper covered in words in a script you do not recognise that you wrote yourself in a fugue state.
7. A length of rope that cannot be cut — you have tried — knotted in a pattern you do not remember tying.
8. A tooth, claw, or bone from something deep-sea that does not correspond to any creature in any bestiary.
9. A mirror that shows reflections a half-second behind reality, found in a ruin beneath the tide line.
10. A name — not your name — that appeared burned into the sole of your foot the morning after the first time you heard the call.

## Legacy

*The weight of a name, a deed, or a blood that the sea remembers*

You are not the first. Your grandmother sailed these waters when Chelish admirals were still drawing maps. Your bloodline carries names that the Shackles remembers — and either you will live up to them, or you will bury them and start again. Either way, you cannot escape what came before you.

The legacy is not always a gift. Sometimes it is a debt. Sometimes it is a target painted on your back. Sometimes it is the reason doors open and sometimes it is the reason they slam shut. What it never is, in your experience, is neutral. The past is not past. It is present and pressing, and it wants something from you.

> **CORE ABILITY** At character creation, establish one true thing about your lineage — a name, a deed, a flag, a ship, an enemy. Once per session, you may invoke this inheritance to establish a fact about the world that connects to it. The GM incorporates it as true.

### Character Questions

-   *What did your ancestor do that still has consequences in the world?*

-   *What do people expect of you because of where you came from?*

-   *What part of the legacy do you wish you could throw overboard?*

-   *What do you know about how your ancestor died, and what does that knowledge make you afraid of?*

### Minor Beats

-   Be recognised not for yourself but for your name, your face, your flag, or a family habit.

-   Meet someone who knew your ancestor personally — and hear something you would rather not have heard.

-   Repeat, word for word, a decision your lineage is known for making, without intending to.

-   Take Minor Reputation fallout from the weight of expectations, obligations, or old debts coming due.

-   Reject a piece of the legacy — burn a letter, refuse a title, abandon a route your family always sailed.

-   Accept a piece of the legacy you previously rejected — take back the name, the flag, the ship's bell.

-   Protect or avenge a person your ancestor wronged, even though you owe them nothing.

-   Be refused something you need because of something your bloodline did before you were born.

-   Find a record, artefact, chart, or journal that belonged to someone in your lineage.

-   Discover that your ancestor's famous deed was a lie, a crime, or a cover-up — and decide what to do with that.

-   Have a younger sailor, deckhand, or aspiring pirate attach themselves to you because of who you come from.

-   Make a choice that directly contradicts what your lineage would have done — and name it as such.

-   Encounter the descendants, enemies, or creditors of someone your family wronged.

-   Claim something — a right of passage, a berth, a title, a fee — on the basis of your name alone.

-   Describe your ancestor in full to another PC — what they did, what they wanted, how they ended.

-   Uphold a family tradition that costs you something: a code, a custom, a rule the world has moved past.

-   Discover that someone else is claiming your family's name, deeds, or flag as their own.

-   Act against a personal interest in order to honour or protect the reputation of your lineage.

-   Invoke your lineage in a public dispute or confrontation — and have the room go quiet because of it. Clear one Reputation stress.

### Major Beats

-   Formally take on a role, title, or burden your lineage is known for — and have it cost you something immediately.

-   Confront the living consequence of your ancestor's most famous act: the person they saved, the port they burned, the enemy they made.

-   Be disowned, denounced, or erased from your lineage by a surviving relative, a faction, or an institution that considered your bloodline an ally. Take Major Reputation fallout.

-   Discover a zenith secret about your lineage: a crime, a bargain, a supernatural obligation, a second family, a living heir to something you thought was yours alone.

-   Take Major Reputation fallout when the legacy works against you — and choose how you respond.

-   Make a choice your ancestor could not, would not, or did not — and let it mean something.

-   Bring a piece of your legacy to a definitive end: retire a ship, pay a debt spanning generations, break a curse, burn a flag.

-   Cause another PC to take fallout because of an obligation, feud, or consequence tied to your bloodline.

-   Be called to arbitrate or represent your lineage in a dispute between old powers — and have your decision bind the outcome.

-   Forge something new that future generations will inherit from you — a ship, a code, a deed, a name.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Ancestor.*** Become the one they will speak of — step into the place your lineage was always building toward, or chart a course so new that the legacy cannot follow.

-   ***The Severance.*** Bury the legacy entirely: give it to the sea, to flame, to silence, or to someone else — and find out who you are with nothing behind you.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Let the weight of what came before finally win — go down not as yourself, but as the latest in a long line of people who could not carry what they were handed.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **LEGACY vs. MONUMENT** *Legacy inherits. Monument constructs. A Legacy character begins the campaign already carrying something of weight. A Monument character begins with nothing and builds toward weight. Both callings are interested in what endures beyond a single life — but Legacy asks what you do with something given, while Monument asks what you do with nothing.*
>
> **LEGACY vs. FELLOWSHIP** *Legacy characters are defined by relationships to the dead — to people who existed before them and whose choices press on the present. Fellowship characters are defined by relationships to the living — to people in front of them right now. Both callings are relational, but they face in opposite directions: Legacy looks back, Fellowship looks at what is here.*
>
> **LEGACY vs. VENDETTA** *Both callings involve a past that will not stay in the past. The difference is agency. A Vendetta character is in active pursuit of a resolution. A Legacy character may not even want resolution — they may just want to understand. Legacy is more ambivalent, more tangled, more capable of genuine moral complexity about the past. Vendetta has a direction. Legacy has a weight.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A signet ring or personal seal belonging to your ancestor, which opens doors it should not still open.
2. A portrait of your ancestor — an oil painting, a sketch, a carved medallion — in which you can see your own face.
3. A ship's log from your ancestor's final voyage, the last few entries water-stained and barely legible.
4. A debt document — unpaid, still legally valid — that an ancestor incurred and that collectors have not yet given up on.
5. A letter from your ancestor to a specific person, never delivered, that you have read enough times to have memorised.
6. An object of great personal significance to your ancestor — not valuable, just important — that was passed to you with no explanation.
7. A weapon your ancestor used that has been repaired so many times it is technically a different weapon, except that it is not.
8. A deed, charter, or agreement granting your family rights over something — a route, a port berth, a stretch of water — that someone else currently controls.
9. A coded message your ancestor sent that was never decoded, which you have been working on for years.
10. Your ancestor's name, tattooed in the old style of your bloodline's home port, which you received without being asked whether you wanted it.

## Fellowship

*The crew as the only thing that matters — and the terrible cost of that being true*

You have never needed much from the world — a good ship, fair winds, and people worth dying for. The crew is everything. Not an abstraction, not a resource, not a means to an end — people, specific people, whose names you know and whose faces you could draw from memory in the dark.

Pirates talk about freedom like it is a solo act. You know better. The only real freedom you have ever felt is the kind you share. The problem — the one that is also the point — is that this makes you vulnerable in a way that treasure hunters and monument builders and revenge-seekers never quite are. You have given people the power to destroy you, and you did it on purpose.

> **CORE ABILITY** Once per session, when a fellow PC or named NPC crewmember takes fallout of any kind, you may immediately remove one stress from any of your own resistances. Watching someone you love get hurt does not break you. It focuses you.

### Character Questions

-   *Who on this crew would you die for without hesitation, and do they know it?*

-   *Who did you lose before this crew, and what does that loss make you do differently now?*

-   *What does this crew give you that you have never been able to give yourself?*

-   *What do you do when you are alone — genuinely alone, no crew, no one watching — that you would never do in front of them?*

### Minor Beats

-   Put yourself between danger and another PC or named NPC crewmember, at cost to yourself.

-   Learn something significant about another PC's history — something they have not told anyone else.

-   Share a moment of genuine rest with the crew: a meal, a night ashore, a calm watch under clear stars.

-   Take Minor Nerve fallout from fear for someone else's safety rather than your own.

-   Make a decision the crew disagrees with and have to live with the friction it creates.

-   Cover for another PC's mistake without being asked and without telling them you did it.

-   Acquire an NPC bond who becomes genuinely part of the crew's life — not a contact, a person.

-   Refuse a personal opportunity — coin, safety, escape — because taking it would mean leaving someone behind.

-   Tell another PC something true about themselves that they needed to hear and did not want to.

-   Grieve, openly or privately, for someone the crew has lost.

-   Take responsibility for something that went wrong, whether or not it was your fault.

-   Let someone on the crew see you frightened, uncertain, or at your lowest.

-   Do something for a crew member that costs you time and resources with no strategic benefit.

-   Argue seriously with another PC about something that matters — not tactics, but values.

-   Be the person who holds things together during a crisis when everyone else is falling apart.

-   Fail someone on the crew in a moment where they needed you, and have to face them afterwards.

-   Celebrate another PC's success more loudly than your own.

-   Bring a stranger into the crew's trust and vouch for them personally.

-   Learn a skill, habit, or piece of knowledge from another PC that changes how you operate.

### Major Beats

-   Risk your life to save another PC or named crewmember when the sensible choice was to let them go.

-   Lose someone from the crew permanently — death, departure, betrayal — and decide what it means for who you are.

-   Discover something about a crewmember that tests your loyalty to its limit — and choose your side.

-   Take Major Nerve fallout as the cost of caring for people in a world that keeps taking them.

-   Hold the crew together through a catastrophe — a shipwreck, a mutiny, a massacre — when it should have broken apart.

-   Be the cause of a serious rift within the crew, however unintentionally, and rebuild what was damaged.

-   Make a sacrifice — material, physical, reputational — that benefits the crew and that you ask nothing for.

-   Choose the crew over your own safety, future, or freedom in a moment where both cannot survive.

-   Accept someone back into the crew's trust after they have genuinely wronged you or someone you love.

-   Build something with the crew that will outlast the voyage — a tradition, a code, a safe harbour, a name.

### Zenith Beats

-   ***The Gift.*** Give everything you have — your life, your future, your freedom, your last resource — for the crew, and make it mean something.

-   ***The Dispersal.*** Watch the crew scatter, fall, or sail on without you — and find that what they carry forward is enough, because you gave it to them.

-   ***The Downfall.*** Lose them — all of them, or the ones that mattered most — and discover whether there is anything left of you when the thing that made you whole is gone.

### DISTINCTIONS & OVERLAPS

> **FELLOWSHIP vs. FREEDOM** *Both callings care about people and their liberation. Freedom cares abstractly — every cage is wrong, every prisoner is a principle. Fellowship cares specifically — these people, this crew, this bond. A Freedom character will fight for strangers on principle. A Fellowship character will fight for the people in front of them and struggle more with the strangers. Both are generous. They are generous differently.*
>
> **FELLOWSHIP vs. MONUMENT** *Both callings involve maintaining relationships and standing within a community. Monument treats those relationships as structural — bricks in a larger construction. Fellowship treats them as intrinsic — the point, not the means. A Monument character will sacrifice a friendship if the structure demands it. A Fellowship character will sacrifice the structure before they sacrifice the friendship. Put both callings in the same party and this tension will surface quickly.*
>
> **FELLOWSHIP vs. LEGACY** *Both callings are relational, but they face in different directions. Legacy looks backward — at the dead, at what preceded the character. Fellowship looks at what is present — the living crew, the current bonds. A Legacy character can play out their entire arc while largely alone with their inheritance. A Fellowship character cannot function without other people. Fellowship is the only calling in this set that is constitutively social — its beats require other PCs to exist.*

### WHAT YOU CARRY

*Roll 1d10 or choose one to determine what you carry to mark your calling.*

1. A small collection of objects belonging to crew members — nothing stolen, things given or left behind — that you keep without telling anyone.
2. A letter you are writing to someone who may or may not still be alive, added to over months, never sent.
3. A list of everyone you have sailed with, living and dead, with a single word next to each name.
4. A small carved figure — rough, clearly self-made — of someone from the crew, which you have never shown them.
5. A tradition from a previous crew that you maintain alone, in private, because stopping it would feel like forgetting them.
6. A scar that a crew member gave you accidentally, which you have told them nothing about and do not blame them for.
7. A song that your previous crew sang together that you continue to sing alone, badly, in the dark.
8. A piece of writing — a poem, a prayer, a set of personal rules — that you produced during the last time you were truly alone and which you have shown no one.
9. An item that belonged to someone you lost before this crew, worn or carried in a way that is not visible until you are by yourself.
10. A running total, kept in your own hand, of every significant thing this crew has done together — not a log, a memorial, written as though someone else will read it after you are gone.

## Piracy

*The shared calling — the beats of the life itself, held in common by every pirate regardless of personal obsession*

This is the life you have chosen — or the life that chose you. The ship, the sea, the prize, the port, the code, and the slow accumulation of enemies and debts and distances that no map fully captures. Every character in the Shackles holds this calling in addition to their personal one, regardless of what else drives them.

Piracy covers what is true of any pirate's story: the things that happen not because of who you are, but because of what you do. At the end of each session, choose two beats from either this calling or your personal calling and tell the GM. The pool is shared — there is no requirement to draw from both, and no hierarchy between them.

### Minor Beats

#### The Ship & the Sea

-   Survive a storm, a rogue wave, a waterspout, or the unnatural weather near the Eye of Abendego.

-   Make a navigational decision under pressure that the rest of the crew has to live with.

-   Spend a night watch alone on deck and arrive at the next port changed in some small way by what you thought about.

-   Run from a superior vessel and make it — or decide not to run and face the consequences.

-   Take Minor Blood fallout from the sea doing what the sea does to bodies.

-   Board a prize vessel and deal with what you find there — not just cargo, but people and the decisions they force.

-   Arrive at an island that is not what the charts said it would be.

#### Ports & the Shore

-   Spend time in a port in a way that costs you something — coin, time, a relationship, a piece of your reputation.

-   Take Minor Loot fallout from the expenses, debts, and decisions that port life generates.

-   Make a contact in a port: a fence, a tavern keeper, a harbour official who owes you or whom you owe.

-   Be recognised in a port — for something you did, something your crew did, or something you did not do but are being credited for.

-   Get into trouble ashore that has nothing to do with your personal calling and everything to do with the life you are living.

-   Hear a rumour in a port that turns out to be true, and wish it had not.

#### The Code & the Politics

-   Invoke the Pirate Code in a dispute — whether or not it resolves in your favour.

-   Deal with another crew: negotiate, threaten, trade, or simply share a dock without incident becoming an incident.

-   Come into contact with the Free Captains' political structure — the Council, a Freebooter's Star, a formal dispute — and leave with your understanding of it changed.

-   Take Minor Reputation fallout from the Shackles noticing something you did, correctly or not.

-   Be asked to do something the Pirate Code permits but that sits badly with you — and choose.

#### The Economics of Plunder

-   Take a prize — and deal with the gap between what you expected it to be worth and what it is actually worth.

-   Divide shares after a job and have the division be the source of friction, however briefly.

-   Lose a prize to weather, pursuit, bad luck, or a decision that seemed reasonable at the time.

-   Take Minor Loot fallout from a score that came in short, a debt that came due, or an expense that could not be avoided.

-   Sell something you stole to someone who knows where it came from.

### Major Beats

-   Survive something that should have killed you — and arrive at the next port knowing the crew saw it happen.

-   Take Major Blood fallout from the sea or from violence, and keep sailing anyway.

-   Come to the formal attention of a hostile power — the Chelish Navy, the Aspis Consortium, a rival Free Captain — in a way that changes what the map of your future looks like.

-   Lose a ship — yours or one you were responsible for — and deal with what that costs in every sense.

-   Make an enemy of another captain or crew in the Shackles who has the standing and the motivation to make your life difficult.

-   Participate in something that changes the political landscape of the Shackles, even in a small way — a Council vote, a power vacuum, a port changing hands.

-   Take Major Reputation fallout from the Shackles reassessing who you are, and spend at least one session living with the new assessment before addressing it.

-   Acquire or lose something significant enough that the crew's material circumstances are genuinely different afterwards — a ship upgrade, a major prize, a catastrophic loss.

-   Do something that gets told as a story in ports you were not present in — and hear a version of it that is not quite right.

-   Face a situation where the Pirate Code and your own judgement point in different directions, and make a choice that has lasting consequences.

## A Note on Beats

Not all beats are things your character would want. A beat that requires you to take fallout, betray an ally, or lose something precious is not a punishment — it is an invitation. When you select it at the end of a session, you are not playing your character. You are acting as the director of their story, indicating what you want to see happen to them. Your character does not know they have beats, any more than they know their cutlass does D6 damage.

You can only have two beats active at any time and cannot hit more than one in any given situation. Choose from either your personal calling or the shared Piracy calling — the pool is combined and there is no hierarchy between them.

## A Note on the Downfall Beat

Every personal calling's zenith includes a Downfall beat. It is always the last option listed, and it is never forced. Selecting it signals that you are ready for your character's story to end not in triumph or transcendence, but in the specific collapse that their obsession made inevitable. The Downfall is not a failure state. In the Shackles, it is often the most honest ending available. It is, in its own way, a form of completion.
